1.0 Overview & Purpose: Identifying Tactical Obstruction in Discovery

In any lawsuit, the pre-trial "discovery" phase is far more than a procedural formality; it is a critical strategic battleground where cases are often won or lost long before trial. Through a series of formal requests, each party seeks evidence from the other to build its case and define the factual narrative. However, this essential process is often exploited by opponents who use improper arguments to obstruct the flow of information.

This training document provides a framework for identifying and understanding these tactics, commonly known as "garbage objections." These are baseless claims asserted not out of a genuine legal concern, but as tactical weapons. They are designed to delay progress, increase costs, exhaust your resources, and ultimately hide unfavorable facts from view.

Recognizing a garbage objection is the crucial first step in a powerful process to gain control of a lawsuit. By identifying and classifying these adversarial tactics, you can begin to dismantle them, create leverage, and force an opponent to comply with their legal obligations. This framework will provide a systematic method for classifying these objections into four primary categories, empowering you to move from a reactive to a proactive posture in the discovery battle.

2.0 Category 1: Clarity & Phrasing Objections

This first category of objections focuses not on the substance of the information sought, but on the discovery request itself. These objections attack the form of the request—how it is written or structured—as a tactic to avoid providing a substantive answer. By claiming a request is confusing, opponents attempt to shift the focus away from their duty to respond and onto the supposed flaws of your drafting.

Core Idea in Plain English: "The request is confusingly written or structured."

Vague, Ambiguous, or Unintelligible (aka "I Have Forgotten How to Use the English Language")

* Definition: This objection asserts that a request is so poorly worded or unclear that the responding party cannot reasonably understand what information is being asked for.
* Common Misuse: This is a classic delay tactic. It is frequently asserted against requests drafted in plain, ordinary English, where a party feigns an inability to understand a straightforward question. The goal is to force you to redraft the request, buying time and creating unnecessary work.
* Counter-Tactic: Challenge this objection directly. Point out that the terms used are common and have well-understood meanings. Stand firm that a reasonable person would understand the request and demand a substantive response.

Compound, Conjunctive, or Disjunctive

* Definition: This objection is used when a single request improperly combines multiple, distinct questions, making it difficult to answer concisely and accurately.
* Common Misuse: This objection has a very narrow, legitimate application for special interrogatories (to prevent circumventing numerical limits). It is almost always improper when asserted against Requests for Production of Documents (RFPs), as a single RFP can properly ask for "all documents" related to a broad topic.
* Counter-Tactic: If asserted against an RFP, state that the objection is inapplicable. If asserted against a special interrogatory, explain that the subparts are all reasonably related to a common theme. Demand a proper response.

Having addressed objections to an inquiry's form, you must now prepare to defend its substantive scope.

3.0 Category 2: Scope & Relevance Objections

This category of objections aims to narrow the field of inquiry by arguing that a request asks for too much information or for information that is not sufficiently related to the legal issues in the case. These are among the most common boilerplate objections used to block access to large categories of potentially important evidence.

Core Idea in Plain English: "The request asks for too much or irrelevant information."

"Fishing Expedition" / Overbroad

* Definition: This is the classic claim that a request is not a targeted search for specific evidence but is instead a broad, unfocused attempt to sift through an opponent's files in the hopes of finding something useful.
* Common Misuse: This objection fundamentally misrepresents the purpose of discovery. The process is intended to allow parties to "fish" for relevant information. Opponents misuse this objection to avoid producing entire categories of documents by pretending that only hyper-specific requests are allowed.
* Counter-Tactic: State that the objection is improper because the standard is not admissibility at trial, but relevance to the subject matter. Explain precisely how the requested information is relevant to the claims and defenses in the case.

Overbroad as to Time

* Definition: This is a more specific version of the overbroad objection, arguing that the request asks for information from an unreasonably long or irrelevant time period.
* Common Misuse: This objection is often asserted without any specific justification. An opponent will claim a timeframe is "overbroad" without explaining why that period is irrelevant to the claims or defenses in the case.
* Counter-Tactic: Justify the time period requested by linking it directly to key events, statutes of limitations, or relevant factual predicates in the case. Demand that the opponent provide a factual basis for their claim of irrelevance.

Irrelevant / Inadmissible

* Definition: This objection argues that the requested information has no logical connection to the issues in the case or that it would not be allowed as evidence if the case went to trial.
* Common Misuse: This is a fundamentally flawed objection in the discovery phase because it applies the wrong legal standard. The threshold for discovery is not whether information is admissible at trial, but whether it is relevant to the subject matter or appears reasonably calculated to lead to the discovery of admissible evidence.
* Counter-Tactic: Reiterate the broad scope of discovery under the law. Explain the clear connection between the requested information and the core issues of the litigation and demand production.

After challenging the scope of the information sought, opponents often shift to claiming that the information is legally protected, which we will explore next.

4.0 Category 3: Privilege & Confidentiality Objections

While some information is legitimately protected from disclosure under the law, these powerful protections are frequently asserted improperly as a shield to hide non-protected, and often damaging, facts. This category of objections claims such a protection applies, and challenging them requires a strict focus on procedural compliance.

Core Idea in Plain English: "The information is legally protected from disclosure."

Attorney-Client Privilege & Work-Product Doctrine

* Definition: These are powerful legal protections for confidential communications between an attorney and client (attorney-client privilege) and for materials prepared specifically for litigation (work-product doctrine).
* Common Misuse: The most common misuse is a blanket assertion of privilege over a category of documents without any supporting detail.
* Proper Procedure & Counter-Tactic: To make a valid privilege claim, the responding party must provide a "privilege log." This log must identify each specific document being withheld and provide enough factual information for the claim to be evaluated. Demand this log. Forcing your opponent to create a detailed privilege log is a victory in itself. It forces them to expend resources and commit, under oath, to the specific basis for their privilege claim, which you can later attack. A general objection of "privilege" without a corresponding log is legally invalid. To counter the common "burden" argument, cite Riddell, Inc. v. Sup.Ct. (2017) 14 Cal.App.5th 755, 772, to establish that there is no "burden" defense to the statutory requirement of preparing a privilege log.

Confidential, Proprietary, or Trade Secret Information

* Definition: This is a claim that the requested information contains sensitive business data—such as financial records, customer lists, or trade secrets—that could harm the company if disclosed.
* Common Misuse: Parties frequently use this objection as an excuse to withhold relevant documents entirely. This is not a valid objection to prevent discovery.
* Proper Procedure & Counter-Tactic: Acknowledge the potential sensitivity, but insist that the proper remedy is not to refuse production, but to negotiate a protective order. A protective order is a court-approved agreement that allows for the discovery of sensitive information while establishing strict rules to safeguard it. Insist on production subject to such an order.

Third-Party Privacy Rights

* Definition: This objection is based on the argument that providing the requested information would violate the privacy rights of individuals or entities who are not directly involved in the lawsuit.
* Common Misuse: When this objection is asserted by a corporation, its application is very limited. Corporate entities have a much weaker privacy right than individuals.
* Counter-Tactic: State that corporate privacy rights are minimal and, as a matter of law, are more easily outweighed by the need for the discovery of relevant information in litigation. Challenge the objection on these grounds and demand production.

Once objections based on the nature of the information are exhausted, opponents often turn to claims about the difficulty of providing it.

5.0 Category 4: Burden & Accessibility Objections

This final category of objections is a common tactic to avoid production by exaggerating the difficulty, expense, or unfairness of providing the requested information. These claims attempt to frame a reasonable discovery request as an unreasonable imposition, shifting the focus from the relevance of the information to the supposed effort required to retrieve it.

Core Idea in Plain English: "Providing this information is too difficult or unfair."

Burdensome and Oppressive

* Definition: This objection claims that the effort required to find, review, and produce the requested information would be unreasonable in terms of time, expense, or labor.
* Common Misuse: This is one of the most frequently used boilerplate objections, often asserted without any factual support. To be valid, this objection requires a specific, factual showing of why the burden is so significant that it amounts to oppression.
* Counter-Tactic: State that the objection is boilerplate and insufficient as a matter of law. Demand a detailed declaration under oath explaining the specific burden. Reiterate that some burden is inherent and expected in the discovery process.

"Equally Available" to Requesting Party

* Definition: This argument asserts that the responding party should not have to produce information because the requesting party can obtain it just as easily from another source, such as public records.
* Common Misuse: This is generally not a valid objection. The fact that information may be available elsewhere does not relieve a party of their duty to respond based on the information within their possession, custody, or control.
* Counter-Tactic: Dismiss the objection as improper. State that it does not relieve the responding party of their legal duty to respond truthfully and completely based on the information available to them.

By understanding these four categories, you can begin to classify and dismantle improper objections. The next step is to recognize the broader patterns of behavior that signal a deliberate strategy of obstruction.

6.0 Red Flags & Escalation Triggers

While classifying individual objections is important, recognizing broader patterns of obstructionist behavior is the key to identifying when objections are being used as a tactical weapon rather than as legitimate claims. These patterns are red flags that should trigger a formal, procedural escalation process to compel compliance.

The primary red flags of tactical obstruction include:

* Pervasive Boilerplate Objections: The discovery responses are littered with a long list of generic, "cut-and-paste" objections asserted against nearly every request, without any specific, factual explanation tailored to that request.
* Improper Privilege Claims: Privilege is asserted broadly over entire categories of documents without providing the legally required privilege log needed to support and evaluate the claim.
* Refusal to Meet and Confer: The opposing party ignores, delays, or refuses to engage in good-faith discussions to resolve the discovery disputes, forcing unnecessary court intervention.

When these red flags are present, you must follow a clear procedural path to force compliance and create leverage:

1. Meet and Confer Letter: This is the first formal step. It is an official communication that identifies each deficient response and invalid objection, explains the legal and factual reasons why they are improper, and demands a full, code-compliant response by a firm deadline.
2. Motion to Compel: If the other side refuses to comply after the meet and confer process, you file a formal request with the court. This motion asks a judge to intervene, rule on the disputes, and order the other party to provide proper responses.
3. Court Order: The successful outcome of a motion is a binding order from the judge compelling the other party to respond by a specific date. This order transforms their discovery obligations into a judicial mandate.
4. Sanctions: If a party violates the court's order, they open themselves up to powerful penalties, or sanctions. These can range from monetary fines to having crucial evidence excluded at trial. The threat of sanctions creates the ultimate leverage to force cooperation.

This progression—from objection, to motion, to court order, to the threat of sanctions—is how you generate the leverage needed to force cooperation, encourage settlement, and ultimately win the discovery battle to help win the war.
