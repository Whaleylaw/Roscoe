1. ## **Introduction:	The	Reasonableness	Requirement	in Medical Damages**

   1. ### **The Problem of Billed Charges in Litigation**

In the aftermath of a personal injury incident, medical bills begin to accumulate. These bills arrive with what appear to be definitive numbers—charges for services rendered, procedures performed, and medications dispensed. For plaintiffs and their counsel, these bills represent the tangible economic harm suffered as a direct result of the defendant's negligence. However, in the complex intersection of healthcare economics and tort law, these billed amounts represent only a starting point for analysis, not a legal conclusion.

The modern personal injury action requires plaintiffs to prove not merely that they incurred medical expenses, but that these expenses were both medically necessary and reasonable in amount. This dual requirement reflects a fundamental principle of compensatory damages: plaintiffs should be made whole, but not receive windfalls disconnected from actual economic loss. The law recognizes a critical disconnect between what healthcare providers initially bill and what represents the true market value of medical services. This disconnect is not merely theoretical—it is rooted in the economic structure of American healthcare, where "sticker prices" bear little resemblance to actual transaction prices.

Consider a common scenario: a patient undergoes an MRI following an automobile collision. The

radiology facility bills $3,500 for the procedure. Medicare, if applicable, might pay $600 for that same service under its fee schedule. A commercial insurer with a negotiated contract might pay $1,200. An uninsured patient might negotiate the bill down to $1,500. Which number represents the "reasonable" value? The answer is neither simple nor self-evident, yet it has profound implications for damage awards, settlement negotiations, and the administration of justice.

2. ### **Historical Context: Evolution of the Reasonableness Standard**

The requirement that medical expenses be "reasonable" is not a modern innovation but rather reflects longstanding principles of damage measurement in common law. Historically, courts have been reluctant to allow recovery of expenses that exceed fair market value, recognizing that to do so would provide plaintiffs with a windfall at the expense of defendants who may have no ability to negotiate or control the amounts charged.

The reasonableness standard gained renewed significance with the dramatic evolution of American healthcare financing over the past half-century. The proliferation of private insurance, the growth of government programs like Medicare and Medicaid, and the development of managed care all contributed to increasingly divergent pricing models. By the late twentieth century, the gap between "billed charges" and "accepted payments" had widened considerably, making the issue of reasonable valuation a central battleground in personal injury litigation.

Several factors contributed to this divergence. First, the elimination of most state-level hospital rate-setting programs in favor of market-based pricing created greater variability in charges. Second, the growth of hospital systems with significant market power allowed some providers to maintain artificially high charge masters—the internal lists of prices for all services and items. Third, the complex interplay of regulatory requirements, insurance contracts, and patient billing practices created an opaque pricing environment where the relationship between charges and costs became increasingly attenuated.

Courts responded to this evolving landscape by refining the evidentiary standards for proving reasonable medical expenses. Early cases simply required plaintiffs to present the bills themselves, operating under a presumption of reasonableness. As the healthcare marketplace became more complex and the divergence between charges and payments more pronounced, courts increasingly demanded additional evidence to establish that billed amounts reflected true market value. This evolution culminated in the modern standard requiring expert analysis to bridge the gap between billed charges and reasonable value.

3. ### **The Necessity of Expert Analysis**

The determination of reasonable medical expenses has evolved from a relatively straightforward inquiry into a highly technical subspecialty requiring specialized expertise. This evolution reflects both the increasing complexity of medical billing practices and the courts' recognition that lay jurors (and even judges) cannot reliably assess the reasonableness of medical charges without guidance from qualified experts.

Several factors make expert analysis essential rather than merely helpful:

**Technical Complexity**: Modern medical billing operates through an intricate system of standardized codes, regulatory requirements, and industry-specific conventions. Understanding whether a bill is properly coded, whether services were appropriately bundled or unbundled, and whether charges

comply with applicable guidelines requires specialized training and experience that lies beyond the ken of lay factfinders.

**Information Asymmetry**: Healthcare providers possess comprehensive information about their own pricing, but this information is not readily accessible to patients, defendants, or courts. Market pricing data exists but is scattered across multiple proprietary databases and public sources. Only experts with access to these resources and the training to interpret them can provide reliable market comparisons.

**Geographic Variability**: Medical pricing varies significantly by geographic region, reflecting differences in practice costs, market competition, and local economic conditions. A charge that is reasonable in Manhattan may be excessive in rural Montana. Proper valuation requires comparing charges to those prevailing in the specific community where services were rendered—an analysis impossible without specialized data resources.

**Temporal Factors**: Medical pricing evolves over time, influenced by inflation, technological changes, regulatory modifications, and market dynamics. A reasonable charge in 2015 may be unreasonable in 2025, or vice versa. Accurate valuation requires historical pricing data indexed to the specific time services were rendered.

**Regulatory Compliance**: Medical billing is governed by a complex web of federal and state regulations designed to prevent fraud, promote transparency, and standardize practices. Determining whether a bill complies with these requirements requires expertise in healthcare regulatory law—knowledge that trial lawyers and judges typically lack.

The modern personal injury practitioner, therefore, faces a professional imperative: engaging a qualified medical billing expert is not a defensive tactic or a cost-cutting measure but a fundamental requirement for meeting the evidentiary burden imposed by law. Failing to do so risks not only reduced damage awards but potential sanctions for failing to adequately support damage claims.

2. ## **The U.S. Medical Billing System: Regulatory Framework and Structural Foundations**

   1. ### **The Necessity of Understanding the Billing Ecosystem**

Before examining how medical bills are evaluated for reasonableness, counsel must first understand the complex regulatory and operational environment in which these bills are created. Medical billing in the United States is not a simple, market-based transaction between provider and patient. Rather, it is the output of a highly regulated, multi-layered system shaped by federal legislation, industry standards, and market dynamics. The modern medical bill is simultaneously a clinical document, a financial instrument, a regulatory compliance record, and a legal claim.

Understanding this ecosystem is essential for several reasons. First, it reveals why billed charges are often disconnected from actual costs or market value—a disconnect that lies at the heart of reasonableness disputes. Second, it explains the origin and significance of the standardized codes and identifiers that make objective analysis possible. Third, it illuminates the regulatory constraints that shape billing practices and create the data trails that experts rely upon. Finally, it provides context for understanding why expert analysis is not merely helpful but necessary—the system's complexity places accurate valuation beyond the capacity of lay factfinders.

2. ### **The Health Insurance Portability and Accountability Act (HIPAA)**

   1. #### **Beyond Privacy: HIPAA's Administrative Simplification Provisions**

When most people hear "HIPAA," they think of patient privacy protections. However, the 1996 Health Insurance Portability and Accountability Act contains provisions far more significant for understanding medical billing: the Administrative Simplification requirements. These provisions revolutionized medical billing by mandating nationwide standardization of transactions, code sets, and identifiers.

Prior to HIPAA, medical billing was characterized by fragmentation and inconsistency. Different payers required different forms, accepted different coding systems, and used different identifiers for providers. This created significant inefficiencies, with providers spending considerable resources simply formatting claims correctly for different payers. More importantly for our purposes, this fragmentation made comparative analysis of pricing nearly impossible.

HIPAA's Administrative Simplification provisions, codified at 42 U.S.C. § 1320d et seq. and implemented through regulations at 45 C.F.R. Parts 160 and 162, mandated the adoption of uniform standards for electronic healthcare transactions. These standards govern not only the technical format of transactions but also the substantive content—specifically, what codes and identifiers must be used.

2. #### **Transaction and Code Set Standards**

HIPAA's Transaction and Code Set Standards (TCS) Rule established specific requirements for how healthcare information is exchanged electronically. The rule designates standard code sets that must be used for encoding data elements in healthcare transactions. For medical billing analysis, three code sets are particularly significant:

**Current Procedural Terminology (CPT) Codes**: Designated as the national standard for reporting physician services and procedures, CPT codes provide a uniform language for describing medical, surgical, and diagnostic services. The American Medical Association maintains and annually updates these five-digit codes, which now number over 10,000. Each code represents a specific service or procedure, defined with precision in the CPT codebook.

The importance of CPT standardization cannot be overstated. Because every provider must use the same code for the same service, charges for identical procedures can be aggregated and compared across different providers, geographic regions, and time periods. This comparability is the foundation for market-based valuation. Without CPT standardization, an expert's analysis would lack the consistent baseline necessary for reliable comparison.

**International Classification of Diseases (ICD) Codes**: While CPT codes describe what was done, ICD codes describe why it was done—the diagnoses that justified the services. The United States adopted ICD-10-CM (Clinical Modification) in 2015, transitioning from the older ICD-9 system. These diagnosis codes contain up to seven characters and provide much greater specificity than their predecessors.

For billing analysis, ICD codes serve two important functions. First, they demonstrate medical necessity

—that services billed were appropriate for the patient's diagnosed conditions. Second, certain services' reimbursement levels depend on diagnosis, making accurate ICD coding essential for proper valuation.

**Healthcare Common Procedure Coding System (HCPCS)**: HCPCS provides codes for services, supplies, and equipment not included in CPT. These codes are particularly important for billing durable medical equipment (DME), ambulance services, and certain drugs and biologicals. HCPCS consists of two levels: Level I (identical to CPT codes) and Level II (alphanumeric codes maintained by CMS for items and services not covered by CPT).

3. #### **The National Provider Identifier (NPI)**

HIPAA also mandated the creation and use of a National Provider Identifier—a unique 10-digit number assigned to each healthcare provider, clinic, and facility. The NPI replaced a confusing array of proprietary identification numbers that different payers had required.

For billing analysis, the NPI serves several critical functions:

**Data Attribution**: The NPI allows charges to be accurately attributed to specific providers while maintaining patient confidentiality. This enables aggregation of charge data by provider type, specialty, and geographic location—all essential elements of market analysis.

**Fraud Prevention**: By creating a universal identifier tied to specific credentialing information, the NPI helps prevent fraud and abuse. An expert's first step in analyzing a bill is often verifying that the NPI corresponds to an appropriately licensed provider authorized to render the services billed.

**Geographic Specificity**: The NPI registry maintained by the National Plan and Provider Enumeration

System (NPPES) includes the provider's practice location, enabling precise geographic analysis. Reasonable charges for a service in one community may be excessive in another, making this geographic specificity essential.

**Specialty Identification**: The NPI record includes taxonomy codes identifying the provider's specialty and subspecialty. This allows for specialty-specific analysis—recognizing, for example, that a general surgeon and a neurosurgeon might appropriately charge different amounts for similar procedures due to differences in training, skill, and market positioning.

3. ### **The Role of Healthcare Clearinghouses**

   1. #### **Function and Purpose**

Between the provider who creates a bill and the payer who adjudicates it lies a critical intermediary: the healthcare clearinghouse. Clearinghouses serve as sophisticated data processing centers that receive claims from providers, validate and "scrub" them for errors and compliance issues, translate them into the standardized formats required by different payers, and transmit them electronically.

Understanding clearinghouses is essential because they play a pivotal role in creating the data that experts ultimately analyze. As claims flow through clearinghouses, data about charges, services, and providers is captured and anonymized. This data feeds the national databases that form the empirical foundation for market-based reasonableness analysis.

2. #### **Claims Processing and Scrubbing**

The clearinghouse's primary function is ensuring that claims submitted by providers meet the technical and substantive requirements for processing. This "scrubbing" process includes:

**Format Validation**: Ensuring claims conform to HIPAA-mandated electronic transaction standards (specifically, the ANSI X12N 837 transaction set for professional and institutional claims).

**Data Completeness**: Verifying that all required data elements are present and properly formatted.

**Code Validation**: Checking that CPT, ICD, and HCPCS codes are valid (correctly formed, not obsolete, appropriate for the service date).

**Compliance Checking**: Verifying compliance with payer-specific requirements and identifying potential coding errors such as unbundling or upcoding.

**Edit Resolution**: Identifying and flagging issues that may cause claim rejection or denial, allowing providers to correct problems before submission.

This scrubbing process serves quality control functions but also creates an audit trail. When a claim passes through a clearinghouse's validation process, it has met baseline standards for proper coding and documentation—information relevant to an expert's technical audit.

3. #### **Data Aggregation and Anonymization**

As clearinghouses process millions of claims, they capture vast amounts of pricing data. This data, when properly anonymized to comply with antitrust regulations and patient privacy requirements, becomes the raw material for the national databases that experts consult. The clearinghouse's role in this data

flow is critical:

**Volume**: Large clearinghouses process claims from thousands of providers across multiple states, creating datasets with sufficient volume for statistically valid analysis.

**Geographic Breadth**: By processing claims from providers nationwide, clearinghouses capture pricing variations across different markets.

**Temporal Currency**: Clearinghouses capture pricing data in real-time, allowing for analysis indexed to specific time periods.

**Standardization**: Because all data passing through clearinghouses has been validated against HIPAA standards, it shares a common structure that facilitates aggregation and comparison.

The transformation of individual claims data into anonymized, aggregated market data represents one of the most important developments in enabling objective billing analysis. Without this data infrastructure, created largely as a byproduct of HIPAA's administrative requirements, modern market-based valuation would be impossible.

4. ### **Antitrust Constraints on Pricing Information**

   1. #### **The DOJ/FTC Healthcare Antitrust Guidelines**

While HIPAA mandated standardization that made pricing comparison possible, antitrust law simultaneously constrained direct pricing discussions among competitors. The 1996 Department of Justice and Federal Trade Commission "Statements of Antitrust Enforcement Policy in Health Care" established ground rules for healthcare provider conduct to prevent anti-competitive behavior.

These guidelines prohibited price-fixing agreements among competing providers and restricted the circumstances under which providers could share pricing information. While these policies served important anti-competitive purposes, they had the unintended effect of reducing pricing transparency. Providers could not directly collaborate to establish "reasonable" rates, and direct price comparison was complicated by concerns about potential antitrust liability.

This regulatory paradox—mandating standardization while restricting direct pricing discussions—created the exact environment where third-party data aggregation became essential. Providers could not share prices directly, but anonymized, aggregated data collected by clearinghouses and other intermediaries was permissible. This made national databases not just useful but necessary for establishing market rates.

2. #### **Implications for Data Collection and Analysis**

The antitrust constraints on pricing information have several important implications for how medical billing experts conduct analysis:

**Reliance on Aggregated Data**: Because individual providers cannot share pricing information directly, experts must rely on databases that aggregate anonymized data from multiple sources.

**Geographic Specificity**: Since market definition is critical in antitrust analysis, pricing data must be analyzed with geographic precision—comparing charges within defined market areas rather than using broad national averages.

**Temporal Accuracy**: Pricing data must reflect the actual time period when services were rendered, as prices may change over time in response to market conditions.

**Statistical Validity**: Data sets must be sufficiently large to provide statistically valid comparisons while maintaining anonymity of individual providers.

Understanding these regulatory constraints explains why expert analysis relies on specific types of data sources and why certain methodological choices (such as focusing on percentile analysis within defined geographic markets) are not arbitrary but reflect the legal framework within which healthcare markets operate.

5. ### **The Affordable Care Act and Recent Developments**

   1. #### **ICD-10 Transition and Enhanced Documentation**

The 2010 Patient Protection and Affordable Care Act (ACA) introduced numerous changes affecting medical billing, though many operated indirectly through regulatory implementation rather than statutory mandate. One significant change was acceleration of the transition from ICD-9 to ICD-10 diagnostic coding.

ICD-10-CM, implemented in October 2015, expanded the universe of diagnosis codes from approximately 14,000 to over 70,000. This dramatic increase in specificity has both benefits and challenges for billing analysis. Enhanced specificity allows for more precise determination of medical necessity and appropriate service levels. However, it also increases the complexity of coding compliance and creates more opportunities for coding errors that may affect billing accuracy.

2. #### **Transparency and Price Disclosure Requirements**

The ACA and subsequent regulations have also moved toward greater price transparency, though implementation has been gradual and sometimes contested. Most significantly for billing analysis, hospitals were required to make their "chargemasters"—the comprehensive lists of charges for all services and items—publicly available.

Later regulatory action, particularly the Trump Administration's price transparency rules finalized in 2019 and implemented in 2021, required hospitals to disclose not only charges but also negotiated rates with different payers. These requirements create additional data sources that experts can use for market validation, though utilization of this data has been uneven due to compliance issues and varying disclosure formats.

3. #### **Value-Based Payment Models**

The ACA accelerated the shift from volume-based to value-based payment models through initiatives like Accountable Care Organizations (ACOs), bundled payments, and quality metrics affecting reimbursement. While these models primarily affect how providers are paid by government programs and some commercial payers, they influence broader pricing patterns.

For billing analysis, the proliferation of value-based models complicates the determination of "reasonable" charges. When a provider participates in bundled payment arrangements or accepts risk-based payments, the relationship between individual service charges and actual payments becomes

even more attenuated. This reinforces the necessity of expert analysis, as simple comparison of billed charges becomes increasingly inadequate for determining true market value.

6. ### **The False Claims Act and Fraud Prevention**

   1. #### **Historical Background and Purpose**

The False Claims Act (FCA), originally enacted during the Civil War in 1863 and substantially amended in 1986, imposes civil liability on those who knowingly submit false claims for payment to the federal government. In the healthcare context, the FCA has become the primary tool for combating fraud in Medicare, Medicaid, and other federal healthcare programs.

The FCA is relevant to billing analysis because it establishes baseline standards for legitimate billing practices. Common violations prohibited by the FCA include:

**Upcoding**: Billing for a more expensive or complex service than was actually provided. For example, billing for a 60-minute psychotherapy session (CPT 90837\) when only 30 minutes were provided (CPT 90832).

**Unbundling**: Billing separately for services that should be billed together as a package. For example, billing separately for surgical prep, the surgery itself, and post-operative care when these are included in the global surgical package.

**Billing for Services Not Rendered**: Submitting claims for procedures or treatments that were never performed.

**False Certification**: Certifying compliance with regulatory requirements as a condition of payment when such compliance does not exist.

**Illegal Remuneration**: Submitting claims for services that result from kickbacks or other prohibited financial relationships, particularly violations of the Stark Law (physician self-referral law) or Anti-Kickback Statute.

2. #### **Qui Tam Provisions and Enforcement**

The FCA contains unique "qui tam" provisions allowing private individuals (called "relators") to file lawsuits on behalf of the government and share in any recovery. This private attorney general mechanism has made the FCA an extraordinarily powerful enforcement tool, with annual recoveries regularly exceeding $2 billion in recent years.

For billing analysis, FCA enforcement data provides valuable information about problematic billing practices. When an expert audits a bill, one function is screening for coding patterns that might indicate FCA violations. While the expert's role is not fraud investigation per se, identifying billing that deviates significantly from compliance standards is relevant to determining whether charges are reasonable and appropriate.

3. #### **Standards for Legitimate Billing**

FCA jurisprudence has established important principles about what constitutes legitimate billing:

**Actual Services**: Charges must correspond to services actually rendered—not services ordered but not

provided, services scheduled but cancelled, or services contemplated but deemed unnecessary.

**Medical Necessity**: Services billed must have been medically necessary for the patient's condition. This requirement links billing propriety to clinical appropriateness.

**Proper Documentation**: Claims must be supported by adequate documentation in medical records. The healthcare maxim "if it wasn't documented, it wasn't done" reflects this requirement.

**Compliance with Billing Rules**: Charges must comply with applicable coding and billing rules, including CPT guidelines, NCCI edits, and payer-specific policies.

These standards, while developed primarily in the fraud context, are also relevant to determining whether charges are "reasonable." A charge that violates FCA standards is not merely fraudulent but also fails to meet the reasonableness standard for civil damages.

7. ### **Synthesis: How Regulation Enables Expert Analysis**

The regulatory framework described above creates both the necessity and the possibility of systematic billing analysis. The complexity and opacity of the system make expert analysis necessary—lay factfinders cannot navigate these regulatory requirements without guidance. Simultaneously, the standardization mandated by HIPAA and the data aggregation facilitated by clearinghouses make objective, data-driven analysis possible.

This regulatory environment has several important implications:

**Standardization Creates Comparability**: Because all providers must use the same codes for the same services, meaningful price comparison is possible. An expert can confidently state that CPT 99213 billed by Provider A is the same service as CPT 99213 billed by Provider B, enabling direct price comparison.

**Data Infrastructure Enables Market Analysis**: The clearinghouse system and associated databases provide the data foundation for determining prevailing market rates. Without this infrastructure, experts would lack reliable pricing information.

**Compliance Requirements Establish Baselines**: Regulatory requirements for proper coding, documentation, and billing create objective standards against which bills can be audited. An expert's technical review thus assesses not merely whether charges are high but whether they comply with established rules.

**Antitrust Constraints Necessitate Third-Party Analysis**: Because providers cannot directly share pricing information, independent expert analysis using anonymized databases becomes the only practical means of determining market rates.

Understanding this regulatory framework transforms billing analysis from an arcane technical exercise into a coherent methodology rooted in federal law and industry standards. The expert's three-pronged methodology, which we examine in detail later, directly reflects this regulatory structure: technical audit (compliance with regulations), market comparison (using standardized data), and local validation (real-world verification).

3. ## **The	Legal	Standard:	Defining	"Reasonable	and Necessary" Medical Expenses**

   1. ### **The Foundational Principle: Recovery Limited to Reasonable Expenses**

In personal injury litigation, a plaintiff's recovery for past medical expenses represents compensation for economic harm actually suffered. This compensatory principle, fundamental to tort law, contains an important limiting condition: recovery is limited to the reasonable value of necessary medical services, not necessarily the amount billed by providers.

This limitation serves several important purposes. First, it prevents windfalls where plaintiffs would recover amounts they never actually paid or incurred as true economic loss. Second, it protects defendants from liability for charges that exceed fair market value, particularly where providers have no negotiated relationship with the patient and may charge inflated "rack rates" for services. Third, it maintains the compensatory purpose of tort damages, ensuring that awards reflect actual economic harm rather than artificial pricing structures.

The reasonableness limitation is not a penalty against plaintiffs but rather a requirement that damage awards reflect genuine economic reality. When a provider accepts $1,000 as payment in full from an insured patient for a service it billed at $3,000, the true economic value of that service to the provider is

$1,000, not $3,000. The law recognizes this reality and limits recovery accordingly.

2. ### **The Evidentiary Standard: Bills Are Not Self-Proving**

A medical bill, standing alone, is not legally sufficient evidence of its own reasonableness. This principle, now well-established across American jurisdictions, reflects the courts' recognition that billed charges may bear little relationship to true market value or actual costs. The mere fact that a provider sent a bill for $5,000 does not prove that $5,000 represents the reasonable value of the services provided.

This evidentiary rule imposes a burden on the party seeking recovery (typically the plaintiff) to offer additional evidence establishing reasonableness. What constitutes sufficient "other evidence" varies somewhat by jurisdiction, but generally requires one or more of the following:

**Expert Testimony**: Opinion from a qualified medical billing expert regarding the reasonable value of services based on market data and industry standards.

**Comparative Pricing Evidence**: Evidence of what other providers charge for similar services in the same geographic area.

**Accepted Payment Rates**: Evidence of what providers actually accept as payment in full from various payers, including insurance companies, Medicare, or Medicaid.

**Fee Schedules**: Published fee schedules from government programs or commercial payers showing reimbursement rates for similar services.

The requirement for "other evidence" reflects judicial recognition that medical billing is sufficiently complex and potentially arbitrary that lay jurors cannot reliably assess reasonableness without expert guidance or objective benchmarks.

3. ### **Arizona Law as Exemplar**

While the reasonableness requirement is nationally prevalent, Arizona case law provides particularly well-developed articulation of the standard and its application. Several Arizona decisions merit detailed examination as they clarify important principles applicable in many jurisdictions.

1. #### **The Fundamental Rule: Reasonable Amount, Not Billed Amount**

Arizona courts have clearly established that "a plaintiff's recovery is limited to the reasonable amount of medical expenses, not the amount charged." This principle appears repeatedly in Arizona decisions and reflects the fundamental distinction between billed charges and reasonable value.

The Arizona Supreme Court has explained the rationale for this rule: allowing recovery of the full billed amount when providers routinely accept less as payment in full would give plaintiffs a windfall. If a provider accepts $1,000 as payment in full from Blue Cross but bills an uninsured patient $2,500 for the same service, the reasonable value is closer to $1,000 than $2,500. The higher charge reflects billing strategy, not true market value.

2. #### **Bills Are Not Prima Facie Evidence of Reasonableness**

Arizona courts explicitly hold that medical bills "are not prima facie evidence of the reasonableness of expenses." This means that simply introducing a medical bill into evidence does not create a presumption of reasonableness that shifts the burden to the opposing party to disprove. Rather, the party offering the bill retains the burden to prove reasonableness through additional evidence.

This heightened evidentiary standard reflects the courts' understanding of healthcare billing realities. The charges on a bill may reflect a provider's "chargemaster" rates—list prices that few if any patients actually pay. Allowing these charges to be presumed reasonable would distort damage awards and undermine the compensatory principle.

3. #### **Billed Charges Are Not "Usual and Customary"**

Arizona courts have rejected the notion that billed charges are equivalent to "usual and customary charges"—a term often used to describe prevailing market rates. This distinction is critical because some parties attempt to argue that whatever a provider charges is, by definition, "usual and customary" for that provider.

The courts have rejected this circular reasoning. "Usual and customary" refers to what providers in a geographic market typically charge or accept for services, not what any individual provider happens to bill. Determining what is "usual and customary" requires market comparison, not mere examination of individual bills.

4. #### **Reasonable Value Equals Accepted Payment Rates**

Perhaps most significantly, Arizona federal courts have held that "'the 'reasonable expenses incurred for

necessary medical services' are those expenses which the healthcare provider accepts as payment in full.'" This formulation directly links reasonable value to actual transaction prices rather than inflated billed charges.

This principle recognizes economic reality: when a provider consistently accepts $X as payment in full from various payers, $X represents the provider's true valuation of their services. The higher amount the provider might bill is essentially a negotiating position, not a true market price. The law bases recovery on the true market price, not the aspirational billing price.

5. #### **Medical Liens and the Burden of Proof**

Arizona statutes governing medical liens reinforce the reasonableness requirement. Under Arizona Revised Statutes § 33-931 et seq., when a healthcare provider files a lien against a patient's claim or judgment, the lienholder bears the burden of proving their charges are "customary." The statute does not simply allow the lien holder to claim whatever amount is billed; it requires proof that the charges reflect customary rates.

This statutory requirement aligns with the common-law rule for medical damages: bills are not self-proving, and the party claiming the charges (whether plaintiff or lien holder) must establish reasonableness through evidence beyond the bill itself.

4. ### **National Prevalence of the Reasonableness Standard**

The reasonableness requirement is not peculiar to Arizona but reflects a national consensus. According to available data, at least forty-six jurisdictions (states and federal circuits) apply some form of this standard, requiring proof that medical expenses are reasonable before allowing recovery.

Representative jurisdictions include Alaska, California, Colorado, Connecticut, Florida, Idaho, Iowa, Massachusetts, Nevada, New Mexico, North Carolina, Oregon, Texas, Washington, and Wisconsin, among many others. Each jurisdiction may articulate the standard slightly differently, but the core principle remains consistent: plaintiffs must prove the reasonable value of medical services, not merely produce bills showing amounts charged.

5. ### **The "Necessary" Requirement**

In addition to being reasonable in amount, medical expenses must also be medically necessary to be recoverable. This requirement ensures that plaintiffs recover only for treatment that was actually required for their injuries, not for unrelated care or excessive treatment.

1. #### **Definition of Medical Necessity**

"Medical necessity" generally means that the treatment was appropriate for the patient's condition and met accepted standards of medical practice. Treatment is not necessary if it was unrelated to the injury, excessive or duplicative, experimental or unproven, or futile.

Medical necessity is typically proven through treating physician testimony, medical records showing the clinical rationale for treatment, expert medical testimony in complex cases, and published guidelines and standards of care.

6. ### **Practical Implications of the Legal Standard**

The legal standard requiring proof of reasonableness has several important practical implications for personal injury litigation, including extensive discovery regarding medical billing, significant effects on settlement negotiations, and strategic trial considerations regarding presentation of evidence.

4. ## **Expert	Methodology:	A	Systematic	Approach	to Valuation**

   1. ### **The Need for Systematic Methodology**

The determination of reasonable medical expenses is not a matter of intuition or general knowledge but requires a disciplined, systematic approach grounded in data and industry standards. The complexity of medical billing, combined with the significant financial stakes involved, demands a methodology that is objective, replicable, comprehensive, defensible, and transparent.

A systematic methodology serves several important functions beyond simply arriving at a dollar figure. It provides a framework for organizing complex information, ensures that no relevant factors are overlooked, creates a record that can be reviewed and critiqued, and lends credibility to the expert's conclusions by demonstrating a thoughtful, rigorous process rather than arbitrary judgment.

2. ### **Overview of the Three-Pronged Analytical Framework**

Medical billing experts employ a three-pronged analytical framework to determine the reasonable value of medical services. This framework addresses three distinct but interrelated questions:

1. **Technical Conformance**: Do the bills comply with applicable coding rules, billing regulations, and industry standards? (Coding and Compliance Audit)

2. **Market Positioning**: How do the billed charges compare to prevailing market rates in the relevant geographic area? (Geographic-Specific Pricing Analysis)

3. **Real-World Validation**: Do the conclusions drawn from technical and market analysis align with observable local market realities? (Local Market Validation)

This three-pronged approach provides multiple layers of analysis, with each prong serving as both an independent investigation and a cross-check on the others. A charge that passes technical scrutiny but far exceeds market benchmarks raises questions requiring further investigation. Conversely, a charge that appears high in database comparisons but aligns with local market observation may be justifiable.

The three-pronged methodology reflects both the regulatory framework governing medical billing and the legal standards for expert testimony. The technical audit ensures compliance with HIPAA and other regulatory requirements. The market analysis fulfills the legal requirement for evidence of "usual and customary" charges. The local validation satisfies evidentiary requirements for reliability by grounding conclusions in real-world observation rather than purely statistical analysis.

3. ### **Foundational Principles Underlying the Methodology**

Several foundational principles underlie the expert methodology:

**Service-Specific Analysis**: Medical billing analysis is inherently service-specific. There is no single "reasonable rate" for healthcare generally; rather, each service, procedure, and supply has its own market value.

**Geographic Specificity**: Medical pricing varies significantly by geographic region, reflecting differences in practice costs, market competition, and local economic conditions.

**Temporal Accuracy**: Medical pricing changes over time due to inflation, technological changes, shifts in practice patterns, and regulatory developments.

**Specialty-Appropriate Comparison**: Different medical specialties may charge different amounts for similar procedures, reflecting differences in training, skill, overhead, and market positioning.

**Data Source Reliability**: The credibility of expert analysis depends heavily on the reliability of the data sources consulted.

5. ## **The Three-Pronged Analytical Framework**

\[Continuing with detailed analysis of each prong...\]

1. ### **Prong One: Technical Audit of Medical Billing and Coding**

The first prong involves a comprehensive technical audit to ensure that medical bills comply with applicable coding rules, billing regulations, and industry standards. This audit answers a threshold question: Were the services billed coded correctly and in compliance with established guidelines?

The technical audit includes verification of code accuracy, bundling and unbundling analysis, modifier analysis, evaluation and management level assessment, review for medically unlikely edits, compliance with NCCI and CCI edits, and documentation review.

Only after confirming proper coding can meaningful price comparison begin. Analyzing the reasonableness of charges for improperly coded services would produce unreliable results.

2. ### **Prong Two: Geographic-Specific Pricing Analysis**

Once the technical audit confirms that services were properly coded, the second prong analyzes whether the charges are reasonable compared to prevailing market rates. This analysis uses the validated CPT code, date of service, and provider's ZIP code to query national databases, establishing 50th, 75th, and 90th percentile charges for specific procedures in specific geographic regions at specific times.

This market-based approach provides objective, data-driven evidence directly responsive to the legal requirement for proof of "usual and customary" charges.

3. ### **Prong Three: Local Market Validation**

The third prong serves as both a reality check and an additional layer of analysis. While national

databases provide robust statistical evidence, the local validation confirms that conclusions drawn from database analysis align with observable market realities through hospital price transparency data, state healthcare cost databases, Medicare fee schedules, workers' compensation fee schedules, and professional experience.

8. ## **Evidentiary Considerations and Admissibility**

   1. ### **The Daubert Standard for Expert Testimony**

The admissibility of expert testimony in federal courts is governed by Federal Rule of Evidence 702 as interpreted by Daubert v. Merrell Dow Pharmaceuticals, Inc. Understanding this standard is essential because even the most thorough billing analysis is worthless if the expert cannot testify.

Rule 702 requires that expert testimony be based on sufficient facts or data, be the product of reliable principles and methods, and reflect reliable application of those methods. The three-pronged methodology for medical billing analysis is explicitly designed to meet these requirements.

The expert must be qualified by knowledge, skill, experience, training, or education. Relevant qualifications include professional credentials (CPC, CCS, CPMA), educational background, professional experience, and industry involvement. Courts have held that qualification does not require state-specific licensure.

2. ### **Reliability Factors**

Courts assess reliability using Daubert factors: \- **Testing**: The methodology can be replicated by other experts \- **Peer Review**: The approach is accepted in the expert community \- **Error Rate**: The methodology has known limitations but produces consistent results \- **Standards**: The analysis follows industry-accepted standards \- **General Acceptance**: The approach is widely used in the healthcare industry

3. ### **Federal Rules of Evidence 703 and 705**

Rule 703 permits experts to base opinions on facts or data that experts in their field would reasonably rely upon. The nationally published databases used in medical billing analysis are precisely these kinds of sources—they are the same repositories that hospitals, clinics, and insurers consult for their own pricing decisions.

Rule 705 allows experts to testify without first disclosing underlying facts or data, though opposing counsel may explore these on cross-examination. Experts should be prepared to explain their methodology and data sources in detail.

9. ## **Defending	Expert	Testimony:	Anticipating	and Overcoming Challenges**

   1. ### **Neutralizing Daubert Challenges**

The introduction of a medical billing expert will invariably draw legal challenges from opposing counsel seeking to exclude testimony. Common attacks and effective rebuttals include:

**Challenge: Lack of Specialized Knowledge** Rebuttal: Qualification is based on knowledge, skill, and experience, not state-specific licensure. Cite Contreras v. Brown precedent.

**Challenge: Insufficient Data** Rebuttal: Analysis is grounded in plaintiff's medical records and bills, benchmarked against industry-wide data aggregated via the HIPAA-mandated NPI system.

**Challenge: Unreliable Principles/Methods** Rebuttal: Methodology is systematic, based on standardized CPT codes, geographic pricing norms, and market analysis—all grounded in accepted bodies of learning.

**Challenge: Improper Application** Rebuttal: The court's gatekeeper role does not supplant the adversary system. Cross-examination is the appropriate means of attacking admissible evidence. Even

"shaky" evidence goes to weight, not admissibility.

2. ### **Refuting Collateral Source Rule Objections**

The collateral source rule prevents a jury from considering payments a plaintiff received from sources independent of the defendant, such as health insurance. Plaintiffs may object that an expert's opinion on "reasonable value" indirectly introduces evidence of insurance write-offs.

**Effective Response Strategy**:

1. **Distinguish from Collateral Sources**: The expert's testimony is not derived from a collateral source. The opinion is grounded in industry principles and broad market data, not on what any specific insurer paid.

2. **Deploy Key Precedent**: Cite Cameron v. Lowes Home Centers Inc., where the court held that expert testimony on "reasonable and customary expense" does not violate the collateral source rule because estimates "do not derive from a collateral source. They are grounded in purported industry principles."

3. **Deconstruct Lopez Arguments**: Explain that reliance on Lopez is misplaced. In that case, the defendant had already stipulated that billed charges were reasonable, making the issue moot. Here, reasonableness is the central disputed issue.

   3. ### **Addressing Percentile Selection Arguments**

Opposing counsel may argue that using a specific percentile (75th or 90th) is arbitrary. This is a misunderstanding of statistical analysis.

**Response**: The use of established percentiles derived from comprehensive, geographic-specific charge data is a standard statistical tool for identifying outliers, not an arbitrary selection. A charge at the 90th percentile is, by definition, an outlier—exceeding what 90% of providers in the market charge. The selection of which percentile represents "reasonable" is a matter of professional judgment based on the specific facts, but the percentile analysis itself is objective.

10. ## **Practical Applications: From Theory to Courtroom**

    1. ### **Case Study: The Slip-and-Fall MRI**

**Scenario**: Plaintiff sustains a back injury in a slip-and-fall. An MRI is ordered, and the imaging center bills $4,200 for CPT code 72148 (MRI lumbar spine without contrast).

**Expert Analysis**:

**Prong One (Technical Audit)**: Expert verifies that CPT 72148 is the correct code for the documented procedure. Documentation supports medical necessity. No bundling issues identified. Code is appropriate for the service rendered.

**Prong Two (Market Analysis)**: Using the imaging center's ZIP code and the date of service, expert queries national databases. Results show: \- 50th percentile (median): $1,450 \- 75th percentile: $1,950 \-

90th percentile: $2,400

The billed charge of $4,200 exceeds the 90th percentile by 75%, placing it far above typical market rates.

**Prong Three (Local Validation)**: Expert reviews hospital price transparency data for the region, confirming that major hospitals charge $1,600-$2,200 for the same procedure. Medicare's local rate is

$580; commercial insurance typically pays 2-2.5x Medicare, suggesting reasonable commercial rates of

$1,160-$1,450.

**Expert Opinion**: "Based on my three-pronged analysis, the reasonable value of this MRI is $1,950, representing the 75th percentile of market charges. The billed amount of $4,200 exceeds reasonable value by $2,250."

2. ### **Case Study: Complex Hospital Admission**

**Scenario**: Plaintiff undergoes emergency surgery following a motor vehicle collision. The hospital bills

$145,000 for a 5-day admission assigned to DRG 480 (Hip and Femur Procedures).

**Expert Analysis**:

**Prong One (Technical Audit)**: Expert reviews medical records to verify DRG assignment. Confirms principal diagnosis and procedures support DRG 480\. Length of stay is typical for this DRG. No complications or comorbidities that would affect DRG assignment. Assignment is accurate.

**Prong Two (Market Analysis)**: Using American Hospital Directory data and commercial payer benchmarks, expert determines: \- Medicare would pay approximately $15,000 for this DRG at this hospital \- Commercial rates typically range from 1.4x to 2.5x Medicare \- Reasonable commercial rate range: $21,000-$37,500 \- The hospital's charge of $145,000 is approximately 4x the high end of reasonable commercial rates

**Prong Three (Local Validation)**: Expert examines the hospital's published chargemaster data and negotiated rates with insurers. Finds that the hospital accepts $28,000-$35,000 as payment in full from major commercial insurers for this DRG.

**Expert Opinion**: "The reasonable value for this admission is $35,000, representing the high end of negotiated rates that the hospital accepts as payment in full. The billed charge of $145,000 is a chargemaster rate that does not reflect true market value."

3. ### **Settlement Negotiation Strategy**

Medical billing experts' opinions significantly affect settlement dynamics. When an expert determines that reasonable value is substantially below billed charges, defense counsel will use this to discount settlement offers.

**Plaintiff Strategy**: Anticipate expert analysis by conducting preliminary review of bills. Where potential reasonableness challenges exist, consider securing plaintiff's expert early to provide counter-analysis. In settlement negotiations, be prepared to defend billed amounts or adjust demands based on realistic assessment of reasonable value.

**Defense Strategy**: Engage billing expert early in litigation to identify bills likely to be challenged. Use

expert findings to anchor settlement negotiations at reasonable value rather than billed amounts. Present expert analysis in mediation to establish credible valuation baseline.

**Defense Expert Presentation**: \- Establish qualifications and independence \- Explain the disconnect between charges and payments \- Present database analysis showing market comparisons \- Demonstrate how charges exceed reasonable benchmarks \- Show evidence of amounts provider accepts from other payers \- Provide clear opinion on reasonable value

11. ## **Ethical Considerations and Professional Standards**

    1. ### **Expert Independence and Objectivity**

Medical billing experts must maintain independence and objectivity. While hired by one party, experts have obligations to the court and to professional standards. Experts should:

* Base opinions on objective analysis, not party advocacy

* Disclose limitations in data or methodology

* Not alter methodology based on which party retained them

* Decline engagements where conflicts of interest exist

* Maintain professional liability insurance

  2. ### **Disclosure Requirements**

Federal Rule of Civil Procedure 26 requires detailed expert disclosures including: \- Complete statement of opinions to be expressed \- Basis and reasons for each opinion \- Data or information considered \- Exhibits to be used \- Qualifications and prior testimony history \- Compensation arrangements

Experts must ensure disclosures are complete and accurate. Incomplete or misleading disclosures can result in exclusion of testimony or sanctions.

3. ### **Professional Standards and Certifications**

Several professional organizations establish standards for medical billing and coding:

**AAPC (American Academy of Professional Coders)**: Sets standards for coding certification and continuing education

**AHIMA (American Health Information Management Association)**: Establishes best practices for health information management

**NAMAS (National Alliance of Medical Auditing Specialists)**: Provides standards for medical auditing

**HCCA (Health Care Compliance Association)**: Promotes ethical conduct in healthcare compliance Experts should maintain appropriate certifications and comply with professional ethical standards.

4. ### **Avoiding Common Pitfalls**

**Overreaching**: Don't opine beyond your expertise. Medical billing experts determine reasonable charges, not medical necessity or causation.

**Cherry-Picking Data**: Use comprehensive data sources and explain any data limitations rather than selecting only supportive information.

**Ignoring Contradictory Evidence**: Address evidence that may undermine conclusions rather than ignoring it.

**Advocacy vs. Objectivity**: Remember your role is expert witness, not advocate. Present objective analysis even if it doesn't fully support the retaining party's position.

13. ## **Glossary of Terms**

**ACA (Affordable Care Act)**: The 2010 federal law that reformed healthcare delivery and insurance, including provisions affecting medical billing and price transparency.

**AMA (American Medical Association)**: The professional organization that maintains and publishes

the CPT coding system.

**Ambulatory Payment Classification (APC)**: The payment system used by Medicare for hospital outpatient services, grouping similar procedures for reimbursement purposes.

**Antitrust Regulations**: Federal laws prohibiting anti-competitive practices, including price-fixing among healthcare providers.

**Average Wholesale Price (AWP)**: The published list price for prescription drugs, though actual transaction prices are typically lower.

**Base Units**: In anesthesia billing, units assigned to procedures based on complexity and risk, as determined by the American Society of Anesthesiologists.

**Billed Charge**: The initial amount a healthcare provider bills for a service, which may exceed what the provider will actually accept as payment.

**Bundling**: Billing multiple related services together under a single code, as opposed to billing each component separately.

**Chargemaster**: A hospital's comprehensive list of prices for all services, procedures, supplies, and medications.

**Clearinghouse**: An intermediary that receives medical claims from providers, validates and "scrubs" them for errors, and transmits them to payers.

**Collateral Source Rule**: An evidentiary rule preventing consideration of payments from sources independent of the defendant, such as health insurance.

**Conversion Factor**: In anesthesia billing, the dollar value per unit that, when multiplied by total units, determines the charge.

**CPT Code (Current Procedural Terminology)**: A five-digit numerical code used to identify medical procedures and services, maintained by the AMA.

**Daubert Standard**: The federal standard for admissibility of expert testimony, requiring that testimony be based on reliable principles and methods.

**Diagnosis Related Group (DRG)**: The payment system used by Medicare for hospital inpatient admissions, classifying stays into groups based on diagnosis and treatment.

**Durable Medical Equipment (DME)**: Medical equipment for home use, such as wheelchairs, hospital beds, and oxygen equipment.

**E\&M Codes (Evaluation and Management)**: CPT codes for physician office visits, consultations, and hospital visits, ranging from simple to complex.

**False Claims Act (FCA)**: Federal law imposing liability for knowingly submitting false claims for payment to the government.

**Geographic Practice Cost Index (GPCI)**: Medicare's system for adjusting payments based on regional variations in practice costs.

**HCPCS (Healthcare Common Procedure Coding System)**: Codes used for services, supplies, and equipment not covered by CPT, particularly DME and drugs.

**HIPAA (Health Insurance Portability and Accountability Act)**: The 1996 federal law that, among other things, standardized medical billing codes and identifiers.

**ICD Code (International Classification of Diseases)**: Diagnosis codes used to identify medical conditions, currently in ICD-10-CM format.

**Medical Necessity**: The requirement that treatment be appropriate for the patient's condition and meet accepted medical standards.

**Medicare Fee Schedule**: The schedule of payment rates Medicare uses for physician services and other medical care.

**Medically Unlikely Edit (MUE)**: CMS-published limits on the maximum units of service that would typically be performed in a single day.

**Modifier**: A two-digit code appended to CPT codes to indicate special circumstances affecting how a service was provided.

**National Correct Coding Initiative (NCCI)**: CMS-developed edits identifying code combinations that should not be billed together.

**National Drug Code (NDC)**: A unique identifier for prescription drugs, identifying manufacturer, product, and package size.

**National Provider Identifier (NPI)**: A unique 10-digit identification number assigned to healthcare providers under HIPAA.

**Percentile**: A statistical measure indicating the value below which a given percentage of observations fall (e.g., 75th percentile means 75% of values are lower).

**Prima Facie Evidence**: Evidence that, if believed, is sufficient to establish a fact unless rebutted.

**Qui Tam**: Provisions allowing private individuals to file lawsuits on behalf of the government under the False Claims Act.

**Reasonable and Customary**: The prevailing market rate for medical services in a specific geographic area.

**Relative Value Unit (RVU)**: A measure of the resources required to provide a medical service, used in Medicare's physician payment formula.

**Unbundling**: Improperly billing separately for services that should be billed together under a single code.

**Upcoding**: Billing for a more expensive or complex service than was actually provided.

**Usual and Customary Rate (UCR)**: The typical charge for a medical service in a particular geographic area.

**Workers'	Compensation	Fee	Schedule**:	State-published	schedules	establishing	maximum

reimbursement for medical services in workers' compensation cases.
