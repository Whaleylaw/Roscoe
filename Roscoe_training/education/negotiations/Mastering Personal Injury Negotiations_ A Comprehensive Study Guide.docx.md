## I. Quiz

Instructions: Answer each question in 2-3 sentences, drawing upon the provided source material.

* Explain why insurance companies have an incentive to delay, dissect, and deny claims.  
* Insurance companies operate on a profit model where they aim to take in more in premiums than they pay out in claims. Delaying claims allows them to earn interest on the held premiums, while dissecting and denying claims reduces the total amount they have to pay out, directly increasing their profit margin. This financial incentive drives their approach to claims.  
* Describe the concept of "bad faith law" in the context of insurance claims, providing a comparative example from the source.  
* Bad faith law holds insurance companies accountable for failing to act fairly and in good faith with their policyholders by not attempting prompt, fair, and equitable settlements. For example, Mississippi has "vicious" bad faith laws with high punitive damages, while Tennessee caps punitive damages at 25% over the policy amount, creating less incentive for insurers to settle there.  
* What is a "Hartford letter" and what internal effects does it trigger within an insurance company?  
* A Hartford letter is a communication, typically sent to defense counsel, stating a willingness to settle a case within policy limits, with a warning that failure to respond within a certain timeframe will lead to trial and direct collection from the insured for any excess verdict. This triggers internal roundtable discussions, creates a potential conflict between the insured and their insurer, and often advises the insured to get separate counsel.  
* Discuss the challenges adjusters face in their work environment, according to the source.  
* Adjusters are often overworked and underpaid, managing 50-100 claims monthly and frequently working over 40 hours a week. Many lack formal medical or legal training, despite evaluating complex cases, leading to a high-pressure environment focused on minimizing payouts to meet performance metrics and earn bonuses.  
* Explain the "Colossus" program's primary function and a key limitation related to data entry.  
* Colossus is an AI software used by insurance companies to standardize and automate the evaluation of auto accident injury claims, often systematically undervaluing them by 12-20%. A key limitation is that adjusters have a 0% accuracy rate in data entry into Colossus, frequently leading to undervaluation if demand letters are not meticulously organized.  
* Why is it important for a plaintiff's attorney to avoid undervaluing a claim initially when adjusters set reserves?  
* If a claim is initially undervalued, adjusters set a low reserve, which serves as a budget. To reset this low reserve later requires extensive internal discussions with upper management, causing significant delays and problems in the settlement process. Providing initial high but justifiable numbers can prevent these delays.  
* How can social media and dashcam footage impact liability disputes in personal injury cases?  
* Social media can be used by opposing parties to find inconsistencies or undermine a client's claims, so clients must be warned about its use and not to remove content. Dashcam footage provides objective, visual documentation of an accident, which can be invaluable in establishing fault, contradicting false statements, or resolving liability disputes definitively.  
* Describe the ethical considerations an attorney must address when communicating settlement offers to a client and obtaining consent.  
* Attorneys must provide honest assessments of case value, risks, and benefits, ensuring the client has sufficient information for informed consent. It is unethical to settle a case without explicit client permission, and any communication about potential settlement figures should clearly state whether the attorney has authority to make or accept that offer.  
* What is the "collateral source rule" and how does it typically affect settlement negotiations in states where it is alive and well?  
* The collateral source rule, in states where it is "alive and well," generally prevents insurance defense lawyers from discussing at trial that a plaintiff's medical bills or wage losses were paid by a third party (e.g., health insurance, workers' compensation). This means that despite payments from other sources, the plaintiff is still legally responsible for the full amount of the bills, and those amounts are typically recoverable from the defendant.  
* Explain why "civility" is an important aspect of negotiation, even when dealing with difficult adjusters.  
* Civility in negotiations, even with cynical or stubborn adjusters, is crucial for building rapport and maintaining professional credibility. While adjusters may try to provoke, remaining courteous and prepared can facilitate smoother discussions, avoid burning bridges for future cases, and potentially help overcome cynicism to achieve a reasonable settlement.

## IV. Glossary of Key Terms

* Adjuster Authority: The maximum dollar amount an insurance adjuster is allowed to approve for claim settlements without requiring supervisor approval. This limit varies based on the adjuster's experience and company position.  
* Aggravation of Injury: When a pre-existing injury or condition is made worse or exacerbated by a new incident, allowing for a claim to be made for the worsened state, even if a completely new permanent injury isn't present.  
* Anchoring: A negotiation strategy where a high-value reference point is set for a claim, often by emphasizing significant economic damages, to influence the perception of the claim's worth and lead to higher settlements.  
* Annuity (in settlement): A financial product often used in settlements, especially for minors or complex cases, where a lump sum is converted into a series of guaranteed future payments, providing long-term financial security and sometimes tax advantages.  
* Artificial Intelligence (AI) in Claims: Software programs like Colossus, ClaimIQ, and Claims Outcome Advisor used by insurance companies to standardize and automate the evaluation of personal injury claims. These systems often systematically undervalue claims.  
* Bad Faith Law: Laws that require insurance companies to act fairly, promptly, and reasonably in handling claims. Violations can lead to lawsuits against the insurer for damages beyond the policy limits.  
* Black Box (vehicle data recorder): An electronic device in vehicles that records data immediately before and during a collision, such as speed, braking, and impact forces, providing crucial evidence in accident reconstruction.  
* Collateral Source Rule: A legal principle, applicable in many states, that prevents a defendant from reducing their liability by claiming that the plaintiff's damages (e.g., medical bills, lost wages) were covered by another source, such as health insurance or workers' compensation.  
* Colossus: A widely used artificial intelligence software program in the insurance industry designed to evaluate and standardize auto accident injury claims, often by assigning "severity points" to injuries and other factors.  
* Comparative Negligence: A legal doctrine that reduces a plaintiff's recovery in a personal injury case by the percentage of fault attributed to their own actions in causing the injury. Some states have specific thresholds (e.g., 50% bar).  
* Confidentiality (Attorney-Client): A broad ethical duty of lawyers to protect all information relating to the representation of a client, regardless of its source or format, unless the client gives informed consent or an exception applies.  
* Concession (Strategic): A tactical move in negotiation where one party willingly gives up something of lesser value to achieve a more significant gain or to break an impasse, often used in cases with disputed liability.  
* Contributory Negligence: A legal doctrine, less common than comparative negligence, where a plaintiff is completely barred from recovering damages if their own negligence contributed in any way to their injury.  
* Crashworthiness: A legal concept in product liability where an automobile manufacturer can be held liable for injuries that result from a vehicle's design flaws, even if the defect didn't cause the initial collision but worsened the resulting injuries.  
* CRN (Civil Remedy Notice): A formal written notice required in some jurisdictions (e.g., Florida) as a condition precedent to bringing a bad faith action against an insurer, outlining specific violations and providing the insurer an opportunity to correct the issue.  
* Discretionary Costs: Expenses incurred during litigation (e.g., expert fees, deposition costs) that, in some jurisdictions, may be recoverable by the prevailing party.  
* Duties Under Duress (Colossus): A specific "value driver" in the Colossus program that accounts for activities a person can still perform but only with pain or difficulty, significantly impacting the claim's valuation.  
* Economic Damages: Tangible, quantifiable financial losses directly related to a personal injury claim, such as medical bills (past and future), lost wages (past and future), loss of earning capacity, and out-of-pocket expenses.  
* Eggshell Skull Doctrine: A legal principle stating that a defendant is liable for the full extent of a plaintiff's injuries, even if the plaintiff had a pre-existing condition that made them more susceptible to severe harm, as the defendant "takes the victim as they find them."  
* EOB (Explanation of Benefits): A statement sent by a health insurance company detailing what medical services were covered, what the insurer paid, and what the patient's remaining responsibility is.  
* ERISA Policy (Employee Retirement Income Security Act): A federal law that sets minimum standards for most voluntarily established retirement and health plans in private industry, which can impact subrogation rights for health benefit plan liens.  
* Ethical Red Flags: Situations that indicate a potential violation of professional conduct rules, such as a client confessing to dishonesty or an attorney knowingly making a false statement of material fact.  
* Fact Finder (Attorney as): The role of an attorney in gathering as much relevant information and evidence as possible to present to the decision-makers (e.g., adjuster, jury) in a clear and compelling manner.  
* Fee Scheduled Amounts: Standardized maximum amounts that medical providers can charge for specific services, often mandated by state law or insurance policies, which can impact the recoverable value of medical bills in a claim.  
* Hartford Letter: A specific type of settlement demand letter, particularly noted in Mississippi, where the claimant offers to settle within policy limits, warning that refusal could lead to the insured being personally liable for an excess judgment.  
* IME (Independent Medical Examination): An examination by a doctor chosen by the insurance company to evaluate an injured person's condition, often used to challenge the treating physician's diagnosis, prognosis, or treatment plan.  
* Informed Consent (Client): The ethical requirement for an attorney to provide a client with sufficient information about a proposed course of action (e.g., settlement offer), including risks and benefits, so the client can make a knowing and voluntary decision.  
* IOLTA (Interest on Lawyers' Trust Accounts): A program where interest earned on pooled, nominal, or short-term client funds held in a lawyer's trust account is used to fund legal aid and other charitable causes.  
* Keloid Scarring: A type of raised, thickened scar that occurs after skin injury, often a significant "value driver" in personal injury claims due to its permanent and visible nature.  
* Letter of Protection (LOP): A document, usually signed by an attorney, guaranteeing payment to a medical provider for treatment rendered to a client from any future settlement or judgment, typically used when a client lacks health insurance.  
* Life Care Planner: An expert who assesses the long-term medical, rehabilitation, and personal care needs of an individual with a catastrophic injury, estimating the associated future costs for inclusion in a damages claim.  
* Litigation Adjuster: An insurance adjuster who takes over a claim once a lawsuit has been filed, typically possessing higher settlement authority and more experience with the legal process than pre-litigation adjusters.  
* Low-Balling: An adjuster tactic where an initial settlement offer is made for a significantly lower amount than the claim's actual or reasonable value, aimed at pressuring the claimant to accept quickly.  
* Medicare Set Aside (MSA): A portion of a personal injury settlement or judgment that is "set aside" to pay for future medical expenses related to the injury that would otherwise be covered by Medicare, ensuring Medicare remains a secondary payer.  
* Minimal Impact Soft Tissue (MIST) Injury: A term used by adjusters to describe minor injuries with little to no visible vehicle damage, often leading to undervaluation of claims.  
* Moeller Counsel: Separate legal counsel advised for an insured when a conflict of interest arises between the insured and their insurance company, often triggered by a "Hartford letter" or potential excess judgment.  
* Narrative (Claim): The cohesive, organized, and emotionally compelling story presented by the plaintiff's attorney, detailing liability, damages, and the impact of the injury on the client's life.  
* Non-Economic Damages: Intangible losses in a personal injury claim, such as pain and suffering, emotional distress, loss of enjoyment of life, disfigurement, and loss of consortium, which are harder to quantify monetarily.  
* Nuclear Verdicts: A term used by defense attorneys to describe extremely large jury verdicts, particularly those that far exceed typical expectations or pre-trial offers, often seen as a result of specific plaintiff strategies.  
* PACER (Public Access to Court Electronic Records): An online system that allows public access to federal court records, often used by attorneys to check for client bankruptcies before disbursing settlement funds.  
* Pain Multiplier: A common method used in personal injury claim valuation where non-economic damages (pain and suffering) are calculated as a multiple (e.g., 1.5 to 5 times) of the economic losses (medical bills, lost wages).  
* PIP (Personal Injury Protection): A type of auto insurance coverage that pays for medical expenses and, in some cases, lost wages, regardless of who was at fault in an accident.  
* Puffery: Exaggerated praise or claims, especially in negotiations, that are not meant to be taken literally as statements of fact; distinct from misrepresentation.  
* Punitive Damages: Damages awarded in addition to compensatory damages, intended to punish the defendant for egregious conduct (e.g., drunk driving) and deter similar actions in the future, rather than to compensate the plaintiff.  
* Recorded Statement: A statement given by a claimant or witness to an insurance adjuster, often over the phone, which is recorded and can be used by the insurance company to find inconsistencies or minimize claim value.  
* Reserves (Claim): An estimate of the money an insurance company sets aside to cover the anticipated costs of an individual claim, functioning as a budgeting tool.  
* Statute of Limitations: A law that sets the maximum time after an event (e.g., an accident) within which legal proceedings may be initiated.  
* Subrogation: The right of an insurer to recover money that it has paid out to its insured from a third party who caused the loss.  
* Tolling Agreement: A legal agreement between parties to suspend or pause the running of a statute of limitations for a specified period, often used to allow for extended negotiations without requiring a lawsuit.  
* Trial Guides: A publishing company and resource for plaintiff's attorneys, offering books, seminars, and services on various aspects of litigation and personal injury practice.  
* Trust Account (Attorney): A separate bank account maintained by an attorney to hold client funds, distinct from the attorney's operating funds, to ensure ethical safeguarding of client money.  
* Value Drivers (Colossus): Specific factors or categories of information that, when entered into the Colossus program, significantly increase the calculated value of a personal injury claim.  
* Venue: The geographic location or specific court where a lawsuit is filed or heard, which can significantly impact case value due to local jury pools, legal precedents, and judicial tendencies.  
* Vocational Expert: An expert who assesses an individual's ability to work and earn income given their injuries, education, and work history, often providing reports on lost earning capacity.  
* Walkaway Strategy: A negotiation tactic where one party signals their willingness to cease negotiations and pursue alternative avenues (e.g., trial) if their demands are not met or if the other side is unreasonable.  
* Workers' Compensation Lien: A claim by a workers' compensation insurer against a third-party personal injury settlement or judgment, seeking reimbursement for benefits paid to an injured worker.
