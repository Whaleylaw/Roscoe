# Study Guide: Liens in Personal Injury Cases

## I. Introduction to Liens and Subrogation in Personal Injury

This section covers the fundamental definitions and distinctions between various types of claims on a personal injury recovery, along with the speaker's background and the general scope of the discussion.

### Key Concepts:

* Lien: An interest one party has in the property of another. In personal injury, this is an interest in the plaintiff's recovery (settlement or judgment).  
* Subrogation: The right of a third party (often an insurance provider) to step into the shoes of the injured person and pursue a claim against the tortfeasor to recover payments made for the injured party's medical treatment.  
* Assignment: A contractual agreement where an injured party transfers their right to recover something (e.g., medical bills, settlement advance) to a third party, often a medical provider or litigation funding company. Letters of Protection are a common example.  
* Right of Reimbursement: A claim by a benefit provider (e.g., health insurance, Medicare, Medicaid) against the beneficiary to be repaid directly from their recovery for related treatment costs.

## II. Types of Liens: Statutory, Contractual, and Combination

This section delves into the different categories of liens based on how they are created and why their classification matters for legal strategy.

### 1\. Statutory Liens:

* Definition: Created by specific laws.  
* Examples:Hospital Liens: State-specific, requiring perfection (filing notice with a clerk's office). Often limited by state law (e.g., Tennessee limits to one-third of settlement).  
* Workers' Compensation Liens (State and Federal): Created by statute; typically attach automatically upon payment, not requiring separate filing like hospital liens.  
* Child Support Liens: Can be "sneaky" as they are unrelated to the personal injury itself and require checking with state child support agencies.  
* Medicare/Medicaid Liens: Federally established programs with specific statutory and regulatory schemes governing reimbursement.

### 2\. Contractual Liens:

* Definition: Arise from agreements between parties.  
* Examples:Letters of Protection (LOPs): Agreements where a medical provider treats a patient without upfront payment, expecting payment from the future settlement.  
* Private Health Insurance Plans: Subrogation clauses in insurance contracts.  
* Assignments: Broader term for contractual transfers of rights, including those to litigation funding companies (often at exorbitant interest rates).

### 3\. Combination Liens:

* Definition: Influenced by both contractual terms and statutory limitations.  
* Examples:Federal Employee Health Benefit Plans (FEHBP): Government plans, not governed by ERISA. Federal law preempts state laws, and reimbursement is governed by plan language.  
* ERISA Health Benefit Plans: Governed by the Employee Retirement Income Security Act. Distinction between "self-funded" (employer retains liability, exempt from state insurance laws) and "fully-funded" (employer purchases insurance, may be subject to state laws).

### Importance of Classification:

* Statutory Liens: Require strict compliance with specific statutory language defining rights and responsibilities.  
* Contractual Liens: Governed by contract terms, but subject to broader laws, rules, and regulations governing liens and insurance.

## III. Key Legal Doctrines and Cases

This section outlines important legal principles and landmark court cases that have shaped the handling of liens and subrogation.

### 1\. Made Whole Doctrine:

* Definition: An equitable defense stating that a third party seeking reimbursement cannot recover anything until the injured party has been fully compensated ("made whole") for all their damages.  
* Application: Common law equitable defense, but often disclaimed by statute or contract, especially in ERISA plans. Some state Medicaid programs may incorporate variations.

### 2\. Common Fund Doctrine:

* Definition: An equitable defense arguing that a third party benefiting from a fund created by another's efforts (e.g., an attorney's work) must share proportionally in the costs of procuring that fund (e.g., attorney's fees and costs).  
* Application: Often incorporated into statutory schemes (e.g., Medicare's pro-rata reduction for procurement costs). ERISA plans may disclaim it through explicit language.

### 3\. Key Cases:

* *Coventry Health Care of Missouri v. Nevils* (2017): Established that contractual provisions within FEHBP plans can preempt state law regarding subrogation and reimbursement.  
* *Sereboff v. Mid Atlantic Medical Services, Inc.* (2006): Clarified that an ERISA plan's claim for reimbursement is "equitable" if it seeks to recover "specifically identifiable funds" within the beneficiary's possession and control.  
* *US Airways, Inc. v. McCutchen* (2013): Held that while the Common Fund Doctrine could be a default, explicit contractual terms in an ERISA plan define the equities and can override equitable defenses.  
* *FMC Corp. v. Holliday* (1990): Distinguished between self-funded and fully-funded ERISA plans, holding that self-funded plans are exempt from state laws regulating insurance.  
* *Gallardo v. Marstiller* (2022): Affirmed that Florida Medicaid could recover from settlement amounts representing "payment for medical care," past or future, not limited to past expenses. (Note dissent concerns over this interpretation and impact on beneficiary incentive).  
* *MSPA Claims 1 LLC v. Tower Hill Prime Insurance* (2022): Eleventh Circuit held that a four-year statute of limitations (28 U.S.C. § 1658(a)) applies when a Medicare Advantage Plan brings suit under the Private Cause of Action provision, with the clock starting when the cause of action accrues (e.g., when the MAO paid the medical bills).

## IV. Medicare and Medicaid Liens: Specific Considerations

This section provides detailed information on managing liens from government health programs, emphasizing their unique characteristics and compliance requirements.

### 1\. Medicare:

* Eligibility: Primarily for those 65+ or under 65 with Social Security Disability (SSD) for 24 consecutive months. Younger individuals can qualify via SSD through a parent's work history.  
* Medicare Secondary Payer (MSP) Act (1980): Made Medicare a "secondary payer" to "primary plans" (e.g., tortfeasor's liability insurance).  
* Conditional Payments: Medicare makes payments when a primary plan doesn't pay promptly (within 120 days). These payments are conditional on Medicare being reimbursed.  
* Right of Reimbursement & Private Right of Action: Medicare has a right to be reimbursed and can directly sue the tortfeasor, their insurer, or anyone who received a primary payment (including attorneys) for double damages if reimbursement isn't made.  
* Parts of Medicare:Part A: Hospital coverage.  
* Part B: Physician services coverage.  
* Part C (Medicare Advantage Plans): Private insurance plans funded by Medicare, often include Part D. Have recovery rights similar to traditional Medicare.  
* Part D: Prescription drug plans.  
* Contacting Medicare:Traditional A/B: Benefits Coordination and Recovery Center (BCRC) or Commercial Repayment Center (CRC) (for WC, PIP, MedPay). Plaintiff's attorneys can use the Medicare portal.  
* Part C/D: Contact the private plan directly.  
* Conditional Payment Process:Disputes & Appeals: Allow for contesting amounts.  
* Waivers: Can request full or partial waiver (e.g., for hardship); reviewed by the contractor, can be appealed.  
* Compromise: Can be requested at any time, but cannot be appealed.  
* Final CP Process (42 CFR 411.39): Allows requesting a final demand amount prior to settlement, requiring settlement within 3 days of request. Requires Medicare Secondary Payer Recovery Portal (MSPRP) access.  
* Reporting Requirements (Section 111): Responsible Reporting Entities (RREs) like insurance carriers must report settlements involving Medicare beneficiaries.  
* PAID Act (Provide Accurate Information Directly Act): Requires CMS to provide RREs with Part C and D plan identifying information for the past three years. This information is not directly available to plaintiff attorneys.

### 2\. Medicaid:

* Needs-Based Program: Eligibility depends on meeting medical and financial (asset/income) criteria.  
* State-Run: Federal program, but administered individually by each state (e.g., TennCare, Medi-Cal). State statutes govern subrogation and reimbursement rights.  
* Reimbursement: Entitled to reimbursement for accident-related payments if the beneficiary receives compensation for medical damages.  
* Eligibility Preservation:Spend Down: Rapidly spending settlement proceeds on allowed expenses within a month to remain below financial thresholds.  
* Trusts:Special Needs Trust (SNT): For beneficiaries 64 or younger. Diverts settlement funds so they are not considered countable assets, preserving Medicaid eligibility. Cannot pay for food, clothing, or shelter directly.  
* Pooled/Community Trust: For beneficiaries 65 or older. Managed by non-profit organizations.  
* Communication: Crucial to coordinate with defense counsel and lienholders regarding Medicaid status and planning.

### 3\. Veterans Administration (VA) Liens:

* Reimbursement Rights: VA is entitled to reimbursement for care related to injuries where a tortfeasor, veteran's own insurance (PIP/MedPay), workers' comp, or admiralty claim is responsible.  
* Billing: VA typically does not bill the patient; the local Judge Advocate Office provides amounts.  
* Compromise/Waiver: Can be requested, but typically no reduction in charges.  
* TRICARE: Separate medical benefit program for military families; requires a separate lien search.

## V. Lien Negotiation Strategies and Ethical Considerations

This section focuses on practical approaches to negotiating liens, including legal arguments and ethical duties.

### 1\. General Negotiation Tips:

* Set Client Expectations Early: Inform clients about the concept of liens and potential repayments from the outset.  
* Understand Underlying Debt: Defeating a lien technically (e.g., due to perfection issues) does not eliminate the underlying debt; the client may still owe the money.  
* Maintain Communication: Proactive and transparent communication with lienholders often leads to better results, especially when discussing recovery issues (e.g., low policy limits).

### 2\. Arguments for Lien Reduction:

* Validity and Perfection: Challenge the lien's validity if the underlying debt doesn't exist or if perfection rules weren't followed (e.g., hospital liens not filed correctly).  
* Relatability: Ensure all charges on the lien itemization are directly related to the personal injury case. Unrelated charges should be disputed.  
* Reasonableness of Charges: Argue that billed amounts are unreasonable, especially if they exceed what would be paid by insurance or other benefit programs (e.g., "balance billing" in Tennessee is often disallowed).  
* Pro-Rata Reductions: Advocate for the lienholder to bear a proportional share of attorney's fees and litigation costs incurred in obtaining the recovery (Common Fund Doctrine).  
* Made Whole Sympathy/Hardship: Even if not legally required, explain the client's financial situation and the impact of the lien on their recovery to encourage a reduction.  
* Doubtful Claim: Emphasize the risks of litigation (e.g., low liability, comparative fault) to motivate lienholders to reduce their demand and facilitate settlement.  
* Jury Determination: If a jury specifically allocates a fixed amount for future medical treatment, this amount is binding on CMS for future medical set-asides, even if it's lower than anticipated.

### 3\. ERISA-Specific Negotiation:

* Self-Funded vs. Fully-Funded: This distinction is critical. Self-funded plans are largely exempt from state insurance laws and equitable defenses (Made Whole, Common Fund), governed purely by plan language. Fully-funded plans may be subject to state laws.  
* Obtain Plan Documents: Request the full plan document, summary plan description (SPD), and Forms 5500 from the plan administrator (they have 30 days, 29 U.S.C. § 1132(c)).  
* Review Plan Language:Does the plan explicitly state a subrogation/reimbursement right?  
* Does it identify a specific pool of money (e.g., the tort recovery) to which the interest applies (constructive trust/equitable lien)?  
* Does it expressly disavow equitable defenses like Made Whole or Common Fund? (If not, argue they apply.)  
* Forms 5500 (Schedule A & C): Can indicate if a plan is fully insured (Schedule A) or self-funded (claims administrator on Schedule C). While not always dispositive, they provide strong arguments.  
* Leverage Settlement Timing: Hold off on finalizing settlement until an agreement with the ERISA plan is reached, as the prospect of no recovery can incentivize reductions.  
* *Schrempf, Kelly, Napp & Darr, Ltd. v. The Carpenters’ Health and Welfare Trust Fund*: Illustrates a firm's ability to sue the ERISA plan for its pro-rata share of fees/costs after paying the lien in full, potentially circumventing plan language.

### 4\. Ethical Considerations (ABA Model Rules):

* Rule 1.7 (Conflicts of Interest): Attorneys must avoid conflicts between clients or between a client and a third party (like a lienholder) or the attorney's own interests. Cannot place personal interests or third-party interests above the client's.  
* Rule 1.15 (Safekeeping Funds): Strict liability rule for handling client funds. Requires prompt notification of receipt, proper segregation (trust account), and prompt distribution. If there's a dispute over a lien, the disputed portion must be kept separate until resolution.  
* Rule 4.1 (Truthfulness to Others): Requires honesty in statements to third parties during negotiations. "Puffery" is allowed, but outright lying is not.  
* Rule 5.3 (Responsibilities Regarding Nonlawyer Assistance): Attorneys are responsible for ensuring that the conduct of non-lawyer staff and third-party vendors (like lien resolution services) complies with professional obligations. Over-delegation without proper oversight is a risk.  
* Referral Agreements: Highly problematic if a medical provider is guaranteed a minimum share of the settlement, as it creates an ethical dilemma where the attorney's loyalty may be split between the client and the referrer.  
* Scope of Representation: Clearly define the scope of representation in the fee agreement (e.g., whether it includes resolving liens, other claims like workers' comp or SSD). Do not expand scope informally.

## VI. Medicare Set-Asides (MSAs)

This section focuses on the requirements and complexities of Medicare Set-Asides for future medical care.

### 1\. Purpose of MSAs:

* Compliance Tool: MSAs are the "preferred method" (but not the only legally recognized way) to comply with the MSP Act's requirement that Medicare remain a secondary payer for future medical expenses related to an injury.  
* Burden Shift Prevention: Prevents the injured party from shifting the financial burden of future medical care to Medicare after receiving a settlement for those same damages.

### 2\. When an MSA is Needed:

* Required if a claim for future or permanent medical damages was made by the plaintiff and is being released as part of the settlement.  
* The plaintiff controls the scope of alleged damages; broad claims (e.g., "lifetime medical needs") can trigger MSA requirements.

### 3\. Avoiding an MSA:

* Certification of No Future Treatment: A strong statement from the *treating provider* (not an IME) on letterhead, dated and signed, explicitly stating that the patient has been discharged and no *future care is required* for the specific injuries. No "wiggle room" (e.g., "as needed," "at this time").  
* No Medical Damages Claimed: In wrongful death cases, if only non-medical damages are pursued for living heirs, no MSA would be needed.

### 4\. What an MSA Includes:

* Financial payments for future, Medicare-covered treatment related to the injury or illness that is "reasonably foreseeable."  
* Examples: Office visits, medications (including refills, generic vs. brand name), diagnostic imaging, physical therapy, surgery (including revisions for joint replacements), durable medical equipment (including replacement), pain management injections, implantable devices (including batteries/maintenance).  
* Rated Ages: Can reduce MSA allocation by shortening life expectancy based on a client's overall medical condition, not just the injury.  
* Patient/Doctor Statements: Statements from the patient electing not to have recommended surgery or from the doctor stating they are no longer a candidate *may* help reduce the MSA, but CMS is not legally bound by them.

### 5\. Types of MSAs and CMS Review:

* Lump Sum vs. Structured Annuity: MSAs can be funded with a single payment or through an annuity.  
* Self-Administered vs. Professional Administration: Can be managed by the beneficiary (with or without assistance) or by a third-party professional administrator (whose fees must be funded separately).  
* Evidence-Based (Non-CMS Approved/Non-Submit) MSAs: These products may be used but CMS warns they "may not adequately protect Medicare" and could be deemed "burden shifting," potentially leading to CMS denying payment for future services until the *entire settlement amount* (less procurement costs) is exhausted.  
* CMS Approval:Workers' Compensation: Formal review process exists with specific thresholds (e.g., Medicare eligible with settlement \> $25k; non-Medicare eligible but expected within 30 months AND settlement \> $250k).  
* Liability/No-Fault: No formal CMS review process *yet*, though CMS has indicated plans to expand review.  
* Common Working File (CWF) Changes (2017): Internal CMS system updated to track liability and no-fault settlements and whether an MSA was created. If no MSA, CMS *may* deem the entire settlement amount as primary to Medicare.

### 6\. Ethical Considerations with MSAs:

* Attorneys must fully advise clients on Medicare compliance, document discussions, and ensure clear language in settlement documents regarding MSA allocations.  
* Failure to properly counsel on Medicaid/Medicare eligibility and MSA impacts can lead to malpractice claims.

## Answer Key

* A lien is a third party's legal interest in a personal injury recovery, whether from settlement or judgment. Subrogation, on the other hand, is the right of a third party, typically an insurance provider, to "step into the shoes" of the injured person to pursue a claim against the responsible tortfeasor to recover payments already made for the injured party's medical treatment.  
* A statutory lien is created by specific state or federal laws, such as a hospital lien established by state statute. A contractual lien arises from an agreement between parties, like a Letter of Protection where a patient agrees for a medical provider to be paid from a future settlement. The key difference is the source of the right: law vs. agreement.  
* The Made Whole Doctrine is an equitable defense where a third party cannot recover reimbursement until the injured party is fully compensated for all their damages. While private health insurance plans might be subject to this common law doctrine depending on state law, ERISA plans often explicitly disclaim it in their plan language, making it more difficult to apply.  
* The Common Fund Doctrine is an equitable principle that requires a third party benefiting from a fund (e.g., a settlement) created by another's efforts (e.g., an attorney's work) to share proportionally in the costs of procuring that fund. Medicare's reimbursement process reflects this by statutorily mandating a pro-rata reduction for procurement costs (attorney's fees and litigation expenses).  
* The Medicare Secondary Payer (MSP) Act was passed in 1980, making Medicare a secondary payer for health benefits if a "primary plan" (like liability insurance) is responsible for medical treatment. Conditional payments are payments Medicare makes for accident-related care when a primary plan does not pay promptly; these payments are made on the condition that Medicare will be reimbursed if a third party is found responsible.  
* Medicaid eligibility can be preserved by either a spend down option, where the plaintiff spends settlement proceeds on allowed items within a specific timeframe to stay below financial thresholds, or by utilizing a trust option, such as a Special Needs Trust (for those under 65\) or a Pooled/Community Trust (for those 65 or older), which shelters the funds from being counted as assets.  
* It is crucial to investigate if a plan is "self-funded ERISA" because self-funded plans are exempt from state insurance laws and equitable defenses like the Made Whole and Common Fund doctrines due to federal preemption. This means their right to reimbursement is dictated strictly by the plan's language, often allowing for 100% recovery, unlike fully-funded plans which may be subject to state-specific reductions.  
* A Medicare Set-Aside (MSA) is a financial allocation from a personal injury settlement designated to cover future medical expenses related to the injury that would otherwise be covered by Medicare. It is generally considered necessary when a plaintiff's claim includes future or permanent medical damages that are being released as part of the settlement to ensure Medicare remains a secondary payer.  
* One key element required for a "certification of no future treatment" statement to be effective is that it must come from the actual treating provider (not an IME), be on their letterhead, and explicitly state that the patient has been discharged and no future care is required for the specific injuries, without any qualifying language (e.g., "at this time" or "as needed").  
* Rule 1.15 (Safekeeping Funds) dictates that an attorney must promptly notify the client of funds received, segregate client funds from the attorney's own funds (typically in a trust account), and promptly distribute funds owed to the client or third parties. It is crucial because failure to comply can lead to strict liability for ethics breaches, especially if there is a dispute over lien amounts, requiring the disputed portion to be held in trust until resolved.

## Glossary of Key Terms

* Assignment: A contractual agreement where one party (injured person) transfers their rights to recover something (e.g., medical bills, settlement advance) to another party (e.g., medical provider, litigation funding company).  
* Balance Billing: The practice of a medical provider billing a patient for the difference between the provider's charge and the amount paid by the patient's insurance or Medicare/Medicaid. Often prohibited or limited by state law or Medicare agreements.  
* BCRC (Benefits Coordination and Recovery Center): The Medicare contractor responsible for recovering conditional payments for Medicare Parts A and B in liability cases and where Medicare is seeking direct recovery from the beneficiary.  
* Certification of No Future Treatment: A statement from a treating physician indicating that a patient has been discharged for their injuries and no further medical care is anticipated for those injuries. Used to argue against the need for a Medicare Set-Aside.  
* CMS (Centers for Medicare & Medicaid Services): The federal agency that administers Medicare, Medicaid, and the Children's Health Insurance Program (CHIP).  
* Common Fund Doctrine: An equitable legal principle that allows an attorney who creates a common fund for the benefit of multiple parties to recover a proportionate share of fees and costs from the entire fund.  
* Conditional Payments: Payments made by Medicare for accident-related medical treatment when a primary payer (e.g., liability insurer) has not paid promptly; Medicare expects to be reimbursed for these payments if a third party is responsible.  
* Constructive Trust: A legal concept where a court imposes a trust on property held by one party for the benefit of another, often used to enforce equitable claims to a settlement recovery.  
* Contractual Lien: A lien that arises from a specific agreement or contract between parties, such as a Letter of Protection.  
* Coventry Health Care of Missouri v. Nevils: A 2017 Supreme Court case holding that contractual provisions in Federal Employee Health Benefit Plans (FEHBP) can preempt state law regarding subrogation.  
* CRC (Commercial Repayment Center): The Medicare contractor responsible for recovering conditional payments for Workers' Compensation, PIP, or MedPay cases, where the responsible reporting entity (e.g., insurer) is the primary debtor.  
* Deemer Clause: A provision in ERISA that prevents states from "deeming" a self-funded employee benefit plan to be an insurance company, thereby exempting it from state insurance laws.  
* Doubtful Claim: A negotiation argument where the attorney highlights weaknesses in the client's case (e.g., liability issues, comparative fault) to convince a lienholder to accept a reduced reimbursement.  
* Durable Medical Equipment (DME): Equipment that can withstand repeated use, is primarily and customarily used to serve a medical purpose, is not useful to a person in the absence of illness or injury, and is appropriate for use in the home. Costs often included in MSAs.  
* Equitable Relief: A type of legal remedy granted by a court that requires a party to perform or refrain from a specific act, often based on principles of fairness rather than strict legal rights. Relevant to ERISA claims.  
* ERISA (Employee Retirement Income Security Act of 1974): Federal law that sets minimum standards for most voluntarily established retirement and health plans in private industry to provide protection for individuals in these plans.  
* Evidence-Based MSA (Non-CMS Approved/Non-Submit MSA): An MSA calculation based on medical evidence and projections, but not formally submitted to or approved by CMS. CMS has warned these may not adequately protect Medicare and could lead to denial of future payments.  
* FEHBP (Federal Employee Health Benefit Plans): Health benefit plans for federal employees. These are governmental plans and not governed by ERISA; federal law preempts state law.  
* Final CP Process (Final Conditional Payment Process): A Medicare process allowing parties to request a final conditional payment amount online prior to settlement, requiring settlement within a strict 3-day window of the request.  
* Fully Funded Plan (ERISA): An ERISA plan where the employer purchases an insurance policy to cover employees' health benefits. These plans may be subject to state insurance laws.  
* Gallardo v. Marstiller: A 2022 Supreme Court case concerning Medicaid recovery rights, holding that Florida Medicaid could recover from settlement amounts allocated to "payment for medical care," whether past or future.  
* Hospital Lien: A statutory lien asserted by a hospital on a patient's personal injury recovery for the reasonable value of services provided due to the injury. Often state-specific regarding perfection and limitations.  
* Letter of Protection (LOP): A contractual agreement, typically between a patient, their attorney, and a medical provider, where the provider agrees to treat the patient without immediate payment, with the understanding they will be paid from any future personal injury settlement or judgment.  
* Lien: A legal claim or a charge on the property of another as security for a debt or obligation. In PI cases, it's an interest in the plaintiff's recovery.  
* Made Whole Doctrine: An equitable legal principle stating that a subrogated party (e.g., an insurer) cannot recover payments from an injured person's settlement until the injured person has been fully compensated for all their damages.  
* Medicaid: A joint federal and state needs-based health insurance program for low-income individuals and families.  
* Medicare: A federal health insurance program primarily for people aged 65 or older, younger people with certain disabilities, and people with End-Stage Renal Disease.  
* Medicare Advantage Plans (Part C): Private health insurance plans that contract with Medicare to provide Part A and Part B benefits, and often Part D. They have recovery rights similar to traditional Medicare.  
* Medicare Secondary Payer (MSP) Act: Federal legislation that makes Medicare a secondary payer to other "primary plans" (e.g., workers' compensation, liability insurance, no-fault insurance) responsible for medical costs.  
* Medicare Set-Aside (MSA): A financial allocation from a personal injury settlement or judgment that is used to pay for a Medicare beneficiary's future medical and/or prescription drug treatment expenses that would otherwise be reimbursable by Medicare.  
* MSPRP (Medicare Secondary Payer Recovery Portal): An online portal used for managing Medicare lien resolution processes, including the Final CP Process.  
* PAID Act (Provide Accurate Information Directly Act): Federal law (2020) that requires CMS to provide Responsible Reporting Entities (RREs) with identifying information for Medicare Part C and Part D plans that a beneficiary has participated in for the past three years.  
* Perfection: The process by which a lienholder takes specific legal steps (e.g., filing, recording notice) to make their lien legally enforceable and establish its priority against other claims.  
* PIP (Personal Injury Protection): A component of auto insurance that covers medical expenses and, in some states, lost wages and other damages, regardless of fault.  
* Pro-Rata Reduction: A proportional reduction of a lien amount, often applied for attorney's fees and costs incurred to procure a settlement or judgment.  
* Quantum Meruit: A legal principle meaning "as much as deserved," often used to determine the reasonable value of services rendered when a contract is terminated, such as when a client fires an attorney in a contingent fee case.  
* Rated Ages: A determination by life expectancy underwriters (e.g., life insurance companies) that an individual's actual life expectancy is shorter than their chronological age due to pre-existing medical conditions, which can be used to reduce MSA calculations.  
* Reasonable and Necessary Charges: A standard used to assess whether medical expenses included in a lien are appropriate in amount and directly related to the injury claimed.  
* Right of Reimbursement: A claim by a benefit provider (e.g., health insurance, Medicare, Medicaid) against the beneficiary to be repaid directly from their recovery for related treatment costs.  
* RRE (Responsible Reporting Entity): Typically, an insurance carrier or self-insured entity, required by Section 111 of the MSP Act to report settlements involving Medicare beneficiaries to CMS.  
* Savings Clause (ERISA): A provision in ERISA that "saves" state laws regulating insurance from federal preemption, meaning fully-funded ERISA plans may still be subject to certain state insurance regulations.  
* Section 111 Reporting: The mandatory reporting requirement under the MSP Act where RREs provide information about settlements, judgments, awards, or other payments to Medicare beneficiaries.  
* Self-Funded Plan (ERISA): An ERISA plan where the employer directly assumes the financial risk for providing health care benefits to its employees, rather than purchasing an insurance policy. These plans are generally exempt from state insurance laws due to ERISA preemption.  
* Sereboff v. Mid Atlantic Medical Services, Inc.: A 2006 Supreme Court case clarifying when an ERISA plan's claim for reimbursement is "equitable" and thus enforceable under ERISA.  
* Special Needs Trust (SNT): A trust established for individuals with disabilities (typically under age 65\) to hold assets (e.g., personal injury settlements) without jeopardizing their eligibility for means-tested government benefits like Medicaid.  
* Statutory Lien: A lien created by law, as opposed to by contract or common law.  
* Subrogation: The right of a third party (e.g., an insurance company) to assume the legal rights of another (e.g., the injured party) to pursue a claim against a third party (e.g., the tortfeasor) to recover payments made.  
* Summary Plan Description (SPD): A document provided to ERISA plan participants that summarizes the plan's provisions in an understandable manner, including rights and obligations.  
* Super Lien: A colloquial term, often applied to Medicare, indicating its powerful and broad recovery rights, including the ability to sue directly various parties (tortfeasor, insurer, beneficiary, attorney) for reimbursement and sometimes double damages.  
* TennCare: The state-run Medicaid program in Tennessee.  
* Tortfeasor: The person or entity who commits a tort (a civil wrong) and is therefore responsible for the injury or damages.  
* TRICARE: A healthcare program for uniformed service members, retirees, and their families worldwide. It has federal reimbursement rights similar to Medicare.  
* Trust Account (IOLTA): A special bank account that attorneys use to hold client funds, keeping them separate from their own operating funds, as required by ethical rules (e.g., Rule 1.15).  
* US Airways, Inc. v. McCutchen: A 2013 Supreme Court case holding that an ERISA plan's explicit contractual terms for reimbursement can override equitable defenses like the Common Fund Doctrine.  
* VA (Veterans Administration) Benefits: Healthcare benefits provided to eligible veterans. The VA has a right to recover payments for injury-related care from responsible third parties.  
* Workers' Compensation Lien: A statutory lien asserted by a workers' compensation carrier against a worker's third-party personal injury recovery for medical expenses and lost wages paid due to a work-related injury.
