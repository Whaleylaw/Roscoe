# Count Module: Fraud

## Usage

Add this count when:
- Defendant made false representations
- Defendant concealed material facts
- False stolen vehicle reports
- Insurance fraud affecting claim
- Misrepresentation of facts affecting liability

**Common Scenarios:**
- Vehicle owner falsely reports car stolen after accident
- Defendant conceals evidence
- False statements to police/insurance

## Prerequisites

- Must establish specific false statements/concealment
- Must show materiality of false information
- Must show intent to deceive
- Must show reasonable reliance
- Must show damages resulting from fraud

## Elements of Fraud

1. **Material misrepresentation** - false statement of material fact
2. **Knowledge** - defendant knew statement was false (or reckless disregard)
3. **Intent** - made with intent to induce reliance
4. **Reliance** - plaintiff reasonably relied on statement
5. **Damages** - plaintiff suffered damages as a result

## Template

```markdown
### COUNT [N]: FRAUD - [defendant.name]

[Paragraph Number]. Plaintiff re-alleges and incorporates all preceding paragraphs as if fully set forth herein.

[Paragraph Number]. Defendant, [defendant.name], engaged in fraud by [making false statements / concealing material facts] regarding [describe subject matter - e.g., "the circumstances of the collision," "the ownership and use of the vehicle," "the identity of the driver"].

[Paragraph Number]. Specifically, Defendant, [defendant.name], [AGENT: describe the fraudulent conduct]:

    a. [Specific false statement or concealment 1];
    
    b. [Specific false statement or concealment 2];
    
    c. [Additional false statements as applicable].

[Paragraph Number]. At the time of making said statements, Defendant, [defendant.name], knew them to be false, or made them with reckless disregard for their truth or falsity.

[Paragraph Number]. Defendant, [defendant.name], made said false statements with the intent to deceive [Plaintiff / law enforcement / insurance company] and to avoid liability for the collision.

[Paragraph Number]. [Plaintiff / law enforcement / insurance company] reasonably relied upon Defendant's false statements.

[Paragraph Number]. As a direct and proximate result of Defendant, [defendant.name]'s fraud, Plaintiff has suffered consequent and proximate injury, including:

    a. Delay in receiving compensation for injuries;
    
    b. Additional costs in pursuing the claim;
    
    c. Denial or delay of insurance coverage;
    
    d. Time and resources expended to uncover the truth.

[Paragraph Number]. Defendant, [defendant.name]'s conduct was willful, wanton, and malicious, entitling Plaintiff to punitive damages above and beyond compensatory damages.
```

## Stolen Vehicle Fraud Specific Language

When owner falsely reports vehicle stolen:

```markdown
[Paragraph Number]. Following the collision, Defendant, [defendant.owner.name], filed a false stolen vehicle report with [law enforcement agency].

[Paragraph Number]. In the stolen vehicle report, Defendant, [defendant.owner.name], falsely stated that the vehicle had been stolen when in fact Defendant knew that [defendant.driver.name] was operating the vehicle with Defendant's knowledge and/or permission.

[Paragraph Number]. The following evidence demonstrates that the stolen vehicle report was false:

    a. [Evidence item 1 - e.g., "The vehicle required a key fob to start and could not be hotwired"];
    
    b. [Evidence item 2 - e.g., "Defendant initially claimed to have only one set of keys but later admitted to having multiple sets"];
    
    c. [Evidence item 3 - e.g., "Phone records show Defendant communicated with the driver before and after the collision"];
    
    d. [Additional evidence].

[Paragraph Number]. Defendant, [defendant.owner.name], filed the false stolen vehicle report in an attempt to avoid insurance liability and to shield [himself/herself] from negligent entrustment liability.
```

## Evidence to Gather

- Police reports (original and supplemental)
- Statements made to insurance
- Written statements/affidavits
- Phone records
- Text messages
- Social media posts
- Vehicle forensics (ignition, key evidence)
- Witness statements
- Surveillance footage
- Prior relationship between owner and driver
