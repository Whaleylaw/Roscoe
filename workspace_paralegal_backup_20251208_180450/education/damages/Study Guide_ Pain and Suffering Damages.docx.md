## Pain and Suffering Damages: A Comprehensive Study Guide

This study guide covers key aspects of pain and suffering damages in personal injury cases, drawing insights from medical and legal perspectives.

### Section 1: Doctor's Insights on Pain and Suffering

Key Concepts:

* Nature of Pain: Pain is a protective mechanism, not always an accurate measure of tissue damage. It can be modulated by somatic, psychological, and social factors.  
* Types of Pain:Nociceptive Pain: Detected in soft tissues (muscles, skin) by special receptors, sent to the spinal cord and brain. Can be somatic (soft tissue) or visceral (internal organs, e.g., kidney stones). Scar tissue itself does not have pain fibers.  
* Neuropathic Pain: Caused by abnormal functioning of nerves, leading to severe and disabling burning or stinging sensations (e.g., sciatica, diabetic neuropathy). Diagnosed via EMG and nerve conduction studies.  
* Psychogenic Pain: Associated with depression and anxiety, often a component of chronic pain after an accident. Requires psychiatric or pain specialist involvement.  
* Idiopathic Pain: Pain for which no objective physical cause can be found. More common in those with pre-existing pain disorders or in athletes under pressure.  
* Referred Pain: Pain felt in a body part distant from the actual pathology (e.g., hip arthritis causing knee pain, herniated disc causing calf/foot pain).  
* Phantom Pain: Pain felt in a limb that has been amputated.  
* Pain Perception Modulation: Constant pain can be blunted by the brain over time, leading to reduced perception even if the underlying condition persists.  
* Healing and Perception: Tissues often heal, but the perception of unhealed injury can sustain pain. Reassurance that healing has occurred can reduce pain perception.  
* Litigation's Influence: Litigation can create perverse incentives, as continuing to complain of pain might be seen as advantageous, even if tissue has healed.  
* Arthritis Pain: Not solely bone-on-bone; often mediated by inflammation. Aggravation of pre-existing osteoarthritis by soft tissue injury is medically controversial without intra-articular damage.  
* Impact of Mental Health: Physical pain can exacerbate mental illness, and mental illness can worsen the perception of physical pain and delay recovery.

Medical Records and Treatment:

* Importance of Chronology: Medical records should tell a chronological story.  
* ER Records: Often inaccurate due to rush and boilerplate entries; require careful review.  
* Operative Reports: Most specific and accurate documents regarding damage and procedures, especially if supported by photos/videos. Dictated immediately after surgery.  
* Duplicate Records: "Copy and paste" notes in EHRs can indicate repetitive, possibly inaccurate, documentation.  
* Ancillary Notes: Nursing, physical therapy, and occupational therapy notes can be very informative about patient complaints.  
* Diagnostic Studies (MRIs): While advanced, MRIs often show abnormalities in healthy individuals over 30, making it hard to discern acute injury from pre-existing conditions without comparing to pre-injury images. Follow-up MRIs post-injury may still appear abnormal even after healing.  
* Treatment Course: Unreasonable prolonged treatment without improvement raises questions.  
* Drug Schedules: Understanding the classification of pain medications (Schedule I-V) is important.  
* Painkiller Concerns: Tolerance, dependency, addiction (opioid epidemic), hyperalgesia (increased pain sensitivity with long-term use), and withdrawal symptoms are significant issues.  
* Steroid Side Effects: High-dose steroids can cause avascular necrosis (AVN), leading to joint death and potential replacements, even from minor initial injuries.  
* NSAID Risks: Non-steroidal anti-inflammatory drugs (NSAIDs) can affect kidney function in older adults.  
* Joint Replacements: Typical lifespan is about 15 years, but can be longer. Costs vary significantly by geographic area.  
* No Surprises Act: Aims to protect patients from surprise medical bills due to out-of-network providers in emergency or in-network facility settings.

### Section 2: Insurance Claims Evaluation

Key Concepts:

* Adjuster Role: Overworked, underpaid individuals managing 50-100 claims. They evaluate claims, assess liability, request documentation, value claims, and negotiate settlements.  
* Early Communication: Crucial to communicate with adjusters early to influence reserve setting.  
* Reserves: Money set aside by insurance companies to pay claims. Low initial reserves can lead to delays and increased scrutiny if the claim value increases significantly later.  
* Insurance Company Motivation: Delaying claims allows them to earn interest on held funds. They also incentivize adjusters to minimize payouts.  
* Attorney Representation: Claims represented by attorneys generally result in higher payouts than unrepresented claims.  
* Colossus Software: AI program used by insurance companies to evaluate auto accident claims, standardizing valuations and often intentionally underpaying.  
* Functionality: Assigns "severity points" based on diagnoses and factors (12,500 factors, 720 diagnoses, limited to first 60 factors and 7 diagnoses per demand).  
* Data Inputs: Medical diagnoses, treatment duration, pain ratings, dissection sheets.  
* Tracking: Tracks county, settlement history, trial verdicts, and attorney profiles (willingness to go to trial, success rates) to generate negotiation advice.  
* Accuracy Issues: Adjuster data entry into Colossus is often inaccurate (0% reported accuracy).  
* Excluded Injuries: Colossus does not evaluate approximately 24 categories of injuries (e.g., death, brain injuries, spinal cord damage, scarring, PTSD), requiring human adjuster evaluation.  
* Value Drivers (for adjusters/Colossus):Statutory violations (speeding, wrong way)  
* Black box data from vehicles  
* Traffic signal timing  
* Video evidence of accident  
* Grotesque photos of injuries (especially ER photos)  
* Accident reconstruction reports  
* Photos of vehicle undercarriage damage (bent metal brackets)  
* Police reports, skid marks, independent witnesses  
* Quantifiable damages (lost wages, work restrictions documented by doctor)  
* Scarring (especially facial, and on legs for females of childbearing years)  
* Inner ear damage

Strategies for Plaintiffs' Attorneys:

* Spoon-feeding Adjusters: Provide well-organized demand packets with all medical codes, bills, and records.  
* Demand Packet Organization: Medical bills on top, related medical records behind, organized by date, Bates-stamped.  
* Framing Facts: Attorneys decide how to frame facts for adjusters and juries.  
* Creating Risk: The goal is to create as much risk as possible for the insurance company to increase valuations.  
* HIPAA Releases: Often broad and open-ended; prefer to obtain records directly.  
* Pre-existing Conditions: Be mindful of internal databases that track prior claims; get information from clients early.  
* Economic Decisions: Understand when it's cost-effective to pursue litigation versus settlement, considering expert costs.  
* Scarring/Disfigurement: Plastic surgeon estimates are good value drivers; particularly high value for female clients of childbearing years, especially for facial or leg injuries.  
* Expert Reports: Doctor's letters quantifying future medical costs and impact on daily life are crucial.  
* Product Liability Claims: Consider if vehicle defects contributed to injury, especially with insufficient insurance coverage. Limit contract if not pursuing.  
* Video Cameras: Dashcam or surveillance footage can be highly impactful.

### Section 3: Proving Conscious Pain and Suffering (Legal)

Key Concepts:

* Definition: Pain and suffering that can be understood and expressed by others (verbal, crying, moaning, wincing, facial expressions).  
* Purpose of Proof: Brings greater accuracy and increases case value.  
* Direct Proof: Testimony by the plaintiff (if available).  
* Indirect Proof:Eyewitnesses: Testimony from those who observed the incident or the plaintiff's immediate reactions.  
* Family Members/Caregivers: Testimony describing the plaintiff's struggles, wound care, emotional impact. Emphasize descriptive terms and "ick factor" to create visceral jury reactions.  
* Experts: Medical examiners (for decedents, based on photos/reports), treatment providers (demonstrating tools, necessity of treatment).  
* Voir Dire (Jury Selection): Important to select jurors receptive to emotional impact, considering their "humanity" (e.g., do they have children/pets?).  
* Evidentiary Concerns:Hearsay: Statements made outside of court offered for the truth of the matter asserted.  
* Hearsay Exceptions: Present sense impression, excited utterance, statement made for medical diagnosis (may need to be to a medical professional).  
* Trial Preparation: Chart out how to prove each piece of pain and suffering, anticipate objections (relevance, prejudice, hearsay), and prepare responses. Jury instructions are an invaluable resource for understanding admissibility.

### Section 4: Plaintiff & Defense Tactics

A. Getting Evidence Admitted (and Keeping Theirs Out):

* Offer of Proof: Essential for all critical evidence. Describes evidence, purpose, relevance, and admissibility grounds. Used in response to objections or affirmatively to put opponent on the spot.  
* Motions in Limine: Pretrial motions to exclude irrelevant, prejudicial, or improper evidence. Know opponent's likely attacks. Be prepared for plan B if motion is denied.  
* Common Defense Motions: Comparative negligence, failure to mitigate damages, assumption of risk (especially in medical malpractice, where informed consent is irrelevant to professional negligence), third-party conduct, positive implications from negligence.  
* Timing: Daubert/Frye motions may require evidentiary hearings.  
* Preservation for Appeal: Renew objections at trial even if ruled on pretrial.  
* Objections at Trial: Calm and professional response. Avoid trivial objections. Court's preference for sidebar or in-front-of-jury objections.

B. Picking the Best Jury:

* Juror Profile: Identify ideal jurors.  
* Voir Dire Strategy: Introduce case themes and concepts early to condition jurors. Encourage self-disclosure of fixed beliefs.  
* Eliminating Unfavorable Jurors:Philosophical Beliefs: Jurors who oppose lawsuits, substantial awards, or believe in "God's appointed time."  
* Biases: Related to client characteristics (smoking, race, body habitus, work history, non-compliance), industry (insurance, healthcare, trucking), specific injury types (low property damage \= no serious injury, delayed treatment).  
* Healthcare Industry: Nurses, sympathy for "healthcare heroes," belief in higher burden of proof, concern for reputation/access/cost of care.  
* Preemptory Challenges: Use strategically after attempting to strike for cause.

C. Conditioning the Jury to Award Significant Damages:

* Language: Avoid "award" (connotes gift); emphasize "righting wrongs," "holding wrongdoers accountable," "preventing future harm."  
* Law: Stress that the law provides for non-economic damages and requires full compensation.  
* Client's "Only Day in Court": Emphasize that this is the only opportunity for client to seek compensation, even if condition worsens.  
* Impact: Highlight media attention, historical precedence, and how verdict might be perceived by others.  
* Life Tables: Use U.S. Life Tables to project future impact of permanent injuries.

D. Strategically Working Up Medical Issues:

* Documentation: Gather all relevant medical records (pre-injury, imaging, bills), analyze for errors, review with client.  
* Excluding Harmful Information: Use motions in limine for irrelevant or prejudicial information (e.g., firearms, drug use).  
* Organization: Bates numbers, chronologies, timelines, billing summaries.  
* "Know the Medicine": Understand client's condition, radiographic findings (e.g., degenerative disc disease does not necessarily mean pain), co-morbidities, medications.  
* Experts: Equip experts with all records. Consider independent examiners if treating physicians are uncooperative.  
* Life Care Plans & Day-in-the-Life Videos: Powerful evidence.  
* Judicial Notice: Use for universally recognized facts like U.S. Life Tables.

E. Creatively Using Exhibits and Demonstrative Aids:

* Simplification: Diagrams, models, animations, PowerPoints clarify complex info (accident reconstruction, medical conditions).  
* Impact: Photographs (not overused, appropriate for time), images of damage (x-rays, MRI). "Seeing is believing."  
* Organization: Visual timelines.

F. Photograph and Personal Journal Evidence Strategies:

* Photos: Powerful but use discerningly.  
* Journals: Refresh memory, present sense impression, real-time application (ADLs, sleep, hobbies), state of mind. Must be consistent with testimony.

G. Maximizing Witness Testimony:

* Third-Party Witnesses: Family, neighbors, friends, coworkers who have no financial stake are highly impactful. Can paint a complete picture of pre- and post-injury life.

H. Tactically Addressing Pre-Existing Injuries and Other Case Weaknesses:

* Transparency: Acknowledge pre-existing conditions; attempts to conceal are risky.  
* "Eggshell Plaintiff" Doctrine: Defendant takes victim as found; liable for all damages, even if pre-existing condition made them susceptible.  
* Differentiation: Use medical records and testimony to distinguish new injury from pre-existing condition, or show aggravation/acceleration.  
* Client Testimony: Crucial for detailing changes in pain, function, mental state.  
* Radiographic Findings: Challenge defense experts who attribute all pain to degenerative changes if client was asymptomatic before.

I. Keys to Powerful Closing Arguments:

* Themes: (e.g., "profits over patients," "one opportunity").  
* Visuals: PowerPoint, video clips, demonstrative aids.  
* Jury Instructions: Review pertinent instructions.  
* Demand Amount: If permitted, ask for a specific amount.  
* "Little Things in Life": Remind jury of lost simple pleasures.  
* Time Value of Money: Break down damages into hours/minutes of suffering.  
* Future: Emphasize client cannot come back for more compensation.

### Section 5: Ethical Considerations

Key Concepts:

* Lawyer Advertising: US Supreme Court (Bates v. Arizona, 1977\) allowed advertising but did not anticipate its current scale and content. Issues with "fight" imagery, exaggerated claims, and potential lack of trial experience among heavy advertisers.  
* Solicitation of Claims: Prohibited direct face-to-face contact, but other methods (e.g., "cappers," sending letters based on police reports) exist and are often permitted.  
* Judicial Misconduct: Trial judges overtly or subtly influencing jury decisions (e.g., cross-examining witnesses, making objections for a party, displaying facial expressions). Requires objection for appellate review.  
* Tripartite Relationship: Insurance defense lawyers serve both the insured and the insurer. The insured's interests take precedence. Ethical dilemmas arise when insurer's interests conflict (e.g., punitive damages not covered by policy).  
* Counterclaims by Defense Counsel: Generally a bad idea as it can boomerang arguments onto the insured and create conflicts (e.g., uninsured motorist claims against own insurer).  
* Social Media:Discovery: Ethical collection of plaintiff's social media activity to assess consistency with claimed disability. Unethical to "friend" plaintiff to gain access.  
* Jury Selection: Permissible to view prospective jurors' *open* social media, but raises privacy concerns.  
* Witness Preparation: Ethical to discuss testimony content and courtroom conduct; unethical to direct testimony or suggest answers the witness would not genuinely offer.  
* Withholding Evidence: Unethical to withhold discoverable evidence and "spring" it at trial.  
* Professional Conduct: Attorneys must be candid and appropriate with the court, opposing counsel, and witnesses. Avoid overly personal or aggressive attacks.  
* Opening Statements/Final Arguments: Must be grounded in evidence. Unethical to overpromise or make statements not supported by evidence.  
* Objecting: Do not overuse objections; avoid appearing to hide information. Strategically decide when to object to inappropriate conduct if it is not harmful to the case.  
* Appeals: Unethical to pursue appeals solely to delay.  
* Surveillance: Defense may conduct surveillance to check consistency of claimed disability. Concerns about invasion of privacy, trespassing, and "roping" (inducing plaintiff to perform inconsistent activities). Security camera footage is now common.

### Quiz Answer Key

* Protective Function of Pain: Pain is essential because it serves as a protective mechanism, signaling that something is wrong with the body. For instance, it tells you if you've broken a bone or torn a tendon, prompting you to seek help and avoid further injury.  
* Scar Tissue and Pain: No, scar tissue itself does not have pain fibers and is therefore not painful. However, scar tissue can indirectly cause pain if it impinges upon or irritates surrounding pain fibers or other sensitive structures.  
* MRI Vision for Superman: Dr. Cooper suggests Superman should have MRI vision because MRIs are far superior to X-rays for discerning soft tissue injuries, which are often the cause of pain. MRI vision would allow for a much better diagnosis of conditions like torn tendons or annular tears.  
* Chronic vs. Acute Annular Tears: An MRI can differentiate an acute tear from a chronic one by showing signs of recent injury. An acute tear would exhibit bleeding, edema, and swelling, similar to a fresh cut on the arm. A chronic tear, however, would show scar tissue formation, indicating an older injury.  
* Perception of Pain: The brain can "blunt" the perception of constant pain by downgrading pain receptors or putting the constant stimuli "out of mind." This allows a person to focus on other external stimuli, even if the underlying physical damage has not resolved.  
* Adjuster's Incentive Structure: Adjusters are often evaluated on performance metrics like claim volume handled without supervisor involvement and may receive bonuses for limiting payouts. This incentivizes them to save money for the insurance provider, potentially at the expense of maximizing claimant benefits.  
* Colossus Program's Limitations: The Colossus program does not fully evaluate catastrophic injuries like death, quadriplegia, or paraplegia. It also excludes specialized conditions such as brain injuries, spinal cord injuries, severe scarring (especially facial), and PTSD, requiring human adjusters to assess these elements.  
* "Eggshell Plaintiff" Doctrine: This doctrine states that a defendant is liable for all damages caused to a plaintiff, even if the plaintiff had a pre-existing condition that made them unusually susceptible to injury. It means the defendant must "take their victims as they find them," ensuring compensation for the full extent of the harm caused.  
* Proving Conscious Pain (Indirect Method): Two indirect methods include testimony from eyewitnesses who observed the incident or the decedent's immediate reactions (e.g., moaning, wincing). Additionally, family members or caregivers can testify descriptively about the decedent's suffering, such as needing wound care or expressing pain.  
* Ethical Concern in Lawyer Advertising: One ethical concern is the exaggeration of legal services or outcomes, such as ads promising lawyers will "fight" or depicting unrealistic scenarios like a lawyer knocking out an opponent. This can mislead the public about the legal process and the actual capabilities of the advertised attorneys.

### Glossary of Key Terms

* Aggravation: A worsened pre-existing medical condition due to a new injury or incident.  
* Annular Tear: A tear in the annulus fibrosus, the outer covering of a vertebral disc, which can cause pain if it involves the outer one-third where pain fibers are present.  
* Anticoagulation: The use of medications (blood thinners) to prevent blood clots, often prescribed after surgery or in individuals with specific risk factors.  
* Avascular Necrosis (AVN): The death of bone tissue due to a lack of blood supply, often caused by high-dose steroids, which can lead to joint destruction.  
* Batson Challenge: A legal challenge to a peremptory strike of a prospective juror, alleging that the strike was made for discriminatory reasons (e.g., race, gender).  
* Biomechanics: The study of the mechanical laws relating to the movement or structure of living organisms, used in accident reconstruction to analyze forces on the body.  
* Boilerplate: Standardized, often generic, language or entries in medical records or legal documents that may not be specific to an individual case.  
* Cappers: Individuals or groups who illegally solicit clients for lawyers, often by acting as intermediaries after an incident.  
* Cauda Equina Syndrome: A serious neurological condition caused by compression of the nerve roots in the lower spinal cord, which can lead to permanent bowel and bladder dysfunction if not treated urgently.  
* Colossus: An artificial intelligence software program used by insurance companies to evaluate and standardize auto accident claims, often resulting in lower settlement offers.  
* Collateral Source Rule: A legal principle that prohibits the reduction of a plaintiff's damages by the amount of benefits the plaintiff received from sources collateral to the defendant (e.g., health insurance, sick leave).  
* Comparative Negligence: A legal principle that reduces the plaintiff's recovery of damages in proportion to their own fault in causing the injury.  
* Conscious Pain and Suffering: Pain and suffering experienced by an individual that is discernible and expressible, even non-verbally, indicating awareness of discomfort. Often a key element of damages in personal injury and wrongful death cases.  
* Cooper's Sign: A diagnostic indicator where knee pain experienced when turning over in bed at night is more typical of a meniscal tear than arthritis.  
* CPT Codes (Current Procedural Terminology): Standardized codes used by medical professionals to describe medical, surgical, and diagnostic services for billing purposes.  
* Daubert Motion/Frye Motion: Pretrial motions challenging the admissibility of expert testimony based on the scientific reliability of the methods used by the expert.  
* Delta V: A measure of the change in velocity of a vehicle during a collision, often used by accident reconstructionists to assess impact severity.  
* Dermatome: An area of skin supplied by a single spinal nerve, used in diagnosing nerve-related pain patterns.  
* Dissection Sheet: A form used by insurance adjusters to record and enter claim factors into the Colossus software system.  
* Duties Under Duress: The performance of normal daily activities despite experiencing pain or limitations, used to document the impact of an injury on a plaintiff's life.  
* EHR (Electronic Health Records): Digital versions of a patient's paper chart, containing medical and treatment histories.  
* Eggshell Plaintiff Doctrine (Eggshell Skull Rule): A legal principle holding that a defendant is liable for all damages caused to a plaintiff, even if the plaintiff had a pre-existing condition that made them unusually susceptible to injury.  
* EMG (Electromyography): A diagnostic procedure used to assess the health of muscles and the nerve cells that control them (motor neurons), often used to diagnose neuropathic pain.  
* EOB (Explanation of Benefits): A statement from a health insurance company detailing what medical services were paid for on behalf of the insured.  
* Exacerbation: A flare-up or worsening of an existing medical condition, distinct from a new injury.  
* Fentanyl: A powerful synthetic opioid, often illicitly mixed with other drugs, known for its high toxicity and role in overdose deaths.  
* Fibromyalgia: A chronic disorder characterized by widespread musculoskeletal pain, fatigue, and tenderness in localized areas.  
* Functional Capacity Evaluation (FCE): A comprehensive assessment of an individual's physical abilities and limitations, often used to determine work capacity or disability.  
* Golden Rule Argument: An improper legal argument where an attorney asks jurors to place themselves in the position of the plaintiff when considering damages.  
* Hearsay: An out-of-court statement offered in court to prove the truth of the matter asserted, generally inadmissible unless it falls under an exception.  
* Hyperalgesia: An increased sensitivity to pain, which can paradoxically be caused by long-term use of opioid painkillers.  
* Idiopathic Pain: Pain of unknown cause, for which no objective physical explanation can be found.  
* IME (Independent Medical Evaluation): An objective medical examination performed by a physician not involved in the patient's care, often requested by insurance companies.  
* Incurred But Not Reported (IBNR) Claims: Claims that have occurred but have not yet been reported to the insurance company.  
* Intra-articular Injury: An injury that occurs within a joint.  
* Judicial Notice: A court's acceptance of certain facts as true without formal proof, often used for readily verifiable information like life expectancy tables.  
* Keloids: Raised scars that occur due to an overgrowth of fibrous tissue after skin injury.  
* Laminectomy: A surgical procedure to remove part of the bone (lamina) from the back of a vertebra, typically performed to relieve pressure on spinal nerves.  
* Life Care Plan: A comprehensive document outlining the long-term medical, rehabilitative, and supportive care needs and associated costs for an individual with a permanent injury or disability.  
* Litigation: The process of taking legal action, especially in a court of law.  
* Malingering: The intentional production of false or grossly exaggerated physical or psychological symptoms, motivated by external incentives.  
* Meniscal Tear: A tear in the meniscus, a C-shaped piece of cartilage that cushions the knee joint.  
* Motions in Limine: Pretrial motions asking the court to rule on the admissibility of evidence before it is presented at trial.  
* Munchausen Syndrome: A psychiatric disorder in which an individual feigns or induces illness in themselves for psychological gain.  
* NBI (National Business Institute): A provider of continuing legal education (CLE) seminars.  
* Negligence Per Se: A legal doctrine that a defendant is presumed negligent if they violated a statute that was designed to protect against the type of injury that occurred.  
* Nerve Conduction Study (NCS): A diagnostic test that measures how fast electrical signals travel along a nerve, used to assess nerve damage.  
* Neuropathic Pain: Pain caused by damage to or dysfunction of the nervous system.  
* No Surprises Act: Federal legislation effective January 2022, protecting patients from unexpected medical bills from out-of-network providers during emergencies or when treated at in-network facilities.  
* Nociceptive Pain: Pain arising from actual or threatened damage to non-neural tissue and due to the activation of nociceptors (pain receptors).  
* OCR (Optical Character Recognition): Technology that converts different types of documents, such as scanned paper documents, PDF files, or images captured by a digital camera, into editable and searchable data.  
* Offer of Proof: A statement made by an attorney to the court explaining what evidence they intend to present and why it is admissible, often in response to an objection.  
* Opioid Epidemic: A public health crisis characterized by high rates of opioid addiction and overdose deaths.  
* Osteoarthritis: A common form of arthritis caused by wear and tear on joints, typically inherited.  
* Pain Diagram: A visual tool used in medical records where a patient marks the location and type of their pain on a body outline.  
* Per Diem Argument: A legal argument in which a plaintiff's attorney suggests a specific monetary value for each day of pain and suffering.  
* Phantom Pain: Pain perceived in a body part that has been amputated.  
* PIP (Personal Injury Protection): A type of auto insurance coverage that pays for medical expenses and lost wages resulting from a car accident, regardless of who was at fault.  
* Plaintiff: The party who initiates a lawsuit.  
* Plaintiff's Decedent: The deceased individual who was the injured party in a personal injury claim, now represented by their estate or heirs.  
* Proprioceptive Fibers: Sensory nerves that provide information about the position and movement of body parts.  
* Psychogenic Pain: Pain that is primarily psychological in origin or significantly influenced by psychological factors such as depression or anxiety.  
* PTSD (Post-Traumatic Stress Disorder): A mental health condition that's triggered by a terrifying event â€” either experiencing it or witnessing it.  
* Pulmonary Embolus (PE): A blockage in one of the pulmonary arteries in the lungs, typically caused by a blood clot that has traveled from elsewhere in the body (usually the legs).  
* Punitive Damages: Damages awarded in addition to compensatory damages, intended to punish the defendant for egregious conduct and deter similar behavior.  
* Radiculopathy: Pain, numbness, or weakness caused by compression or irritation of a nerve root in the spine (e.g., sciatica).  
* Referred Pain: Pain perceived at a location other than the site of the painful stimulus.  
* Reserves (Insurance): Funds set aside by insurance companies to cover anticipated future claim payouts.  
* Roping: An unethical surveillance technique where an investigator induces a plaintiff to perform physical activities inconsistent with their claimed disability.  
* Rotator Cuff Tear: A tear in one of the muscles or tendons that surround the shoulder joint.  
* Sciatic Nerve: The largest nerve in the body, running from the lower back down each leg, irritation of which causes sciatica.  
* Schedule I-V Drugs: Classifications by the DEA based on a drug's accepted medical use and potential for abuse/dependence, with Schedule I having no accepted medical use and high abuse potential, and Schedule V having the lowest.  
* Somatic Pain: Pain arising from the skin, muscles, bones, joints, and connective tissues.  
* Statute of Limitations: A law that sets the maximum time after an event within which legal proceedings may be initiated.  
* Stocking Glove Hyperesthesia: A neurological pattern of sensory loss or increased sensation that affects the hands and feet in a "stocking and glove" distribution, sometimes used euphemistically to suggest non-physiological pain.  
* Subrogation: The right of an insurer to pursue a third party that caused an insurance loss to the insured.  
* Symptom Exaggeration: The conscious overstating of real symptoms, often assessed using signs like Waddell's.  
* Tens Unit (Transcutaneous Electrical Nerve Stimulation): A small, battery-operated device that delivers electrical impulses through electrodes placed on the skin to relieve pain.  
* Tibial Plateau Fracture: A fracture of the top surface of the tibia (shin bone), often caused by high-energy trauma.  
* Tramadol: A synthetic opioid pain reliever, classified as a Schedule IV drug, generally considered milder than stronger opioids.  
* Tripartite Relationship: The three-way relationship between an insurance company, its insured, and the defense attorney hired by the insurer to represent the insured.  
* Uninsured/Underinsured Motorist (UM/UIM) Coverage: Insurance coverage that protects you if you're involved in an accident with a driver who is uninsured or doesn't have enough insurance to cover your damages.  
* Utilizaton Review: A process used by insurance companies to determine if medical services are medically necessary and appropriate.  
* Visceral Pain: Pain that results from the activation of nociceptors in the internal organs and linings of the body cavities.  
* Voir Dire: The process of jury selection, where prospective jurors are questioned to determine their suitability for jury service.  
* Waddell's Sign: A set of non-organic physical signs assessed during a physical examination, primarily for low back pain, that may indicate symptom exaggeration or a psychological component to pain.  
* Wrongful Death Claim: A lawsuit brought by the family or estate of a deceased person who died due to the negligence or misconduct of another.
