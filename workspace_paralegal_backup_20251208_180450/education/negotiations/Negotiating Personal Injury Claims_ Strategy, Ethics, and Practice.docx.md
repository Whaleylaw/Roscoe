# **Negotiating Personal Injury Claims: Strategy, Ethics, and Practice**

## **Introduction**

The vast majority of personal injury disputes resolve without a verdict. Negotiation—not trial—is where value is most often created or lost. Effective settlement advocacy requires an integrated understanding of insurer economics and workflow, evidentiary development calibrated to legal standards, fluency with automated valuation systems, and steady ethical judgment. Adjusters operate within authority ladders, reserve budgets, and software guardrails; lawyers must present organized, credible proof that fits within those systems yet is compelling to human decision‑makers.

This chapter synthesizes doctrine and practice into a step‑by‑step playbook. Each subsection includes a concrete example to illustrate how the principle operates in real files. A comprehensive glossary appears at the end for quick reference.

## **Core Doctrines / Principles**

### **Insurance Economics and the Reserve System**

Reserves are the carrier’s running estimate of ultimate exposure. The initial reserve anchors internal expectations and sets the adjuster’s practical ceiling. Increasing a reserve later usually requires supervisor approval or a claims "roundtable," creating delay and resistance. Authority ladders (e.g., $15k → $50k → $100k → home office) and calendar‑year closeout pressures compound these dynamics. Adjusters are commonly overworked—managing dozens of active files—and many lack formal medical or legal training, which increases reliance on software and checklists.

**Practice implications**

* **Front‑load high‑value facts** (surgery, permanent impairment, wage loss) with the notice of representation to justify a robust opening reserve.  
* **Time demands** around quarter‑ and year‑end when files are cleared and reserves reset.  
* **Escalate intelligently**: tailor summaries for supervisor review and anticipate internal challenges to reserve increases.  
* **Recognize conflict pressure**: time‑limited, within‑limits demands can create insurer–insured conflicts that lead to appointment of separate counsel for the insured (often called Moeller counsel in some jurisdictions).

*Example—Early Reserve Win:* Within one week of intake, counsel for a bicyclist sends the carrier ER operative reports, photographs of hardware, a wage‑loss letter confirming 14 weeks off, and a liability memo citing the 3‑foot passing statute. The adjuster sets a six‑figure reserve and seeks authority before defense counsel is retained, avoiding months of low‑balling.

**Practice Note (Reserves & Authority)**  
• Send a 1‑page **reserve memo** within 7 days of intake (surgery/permanency/wage loss first).  
• Ask the adjuster for the **reserve‑change workflow** and supervisor contact; calendar quarter‑ and year‑end dates.  
• Avoid low anchors early; every soft number risks a **low opening reserve**.  
• When you deliver major new value (e.g., surgery rec, impairment), request a **roundtable** and include a supervisor‑ready summary.  
• Track authority **tiers** (e.g., $15k/$50k/$100k/home office) and time your asks to scheduled meetings.

### **Fault and Causation**

Liability strength and causation drive settlement value. Negligence per se from statutory violations simplifies duty and breach; comparative negligence can reduce recovery; the eggshell‑skull rule preserves damages despite vulnerability. Causation proof marries mechanism of injury to diagnosis and time course.

**Tools and reminders**

* Scene photographs, measurements, and event‑data recorder (EDR) downloads corroborate fault.  
* Medical causation rests on contemporaneous complaints, objective findings when present, and treating‑physician opinions.  
* In mixed‑fault cases, strategic concessions can unlock value while preserving damages.

*Example—Comparative Fault Managed:* A pedestrian crosses mid‑block at dusk; the driver was speeding while texting. Counsel models verdict ranges at 20–40% pedestrian fault, secures a reconstruction report on stopping distances, and obtains treating‑physician opinions on permanent ankle limits. A reasoned 25% fault concession, paired with strong damages, produces a mid‑six‑figure settlement.

**Practice Note (Liability & Causation Quick Hits)**  
• Lock in **negligence per se** with the statute and citation disposition.  
• Preserve **EDR** data; send tow‑yard and carrier preservation letters day one.  
• Get **911 audio/CAD logs** and canvass business/doorbell cams within overwrite windows.  
• Build a **symptom timeline** (onset within 24–48 hours; continuous complaints).  
• Model **comparative fault** with a simple spreadsheet to frame negotiation bands.

### **Damages Framework**

Compensatory damages include economic (past/future medical expenses, wage loss, diminished earning capacity, household services, out‑of‑pocket costs) and non‑economic (pain, suffering, loss of normal life, disfigurement, emotional distress). Future care requires medical foundation and reasonable, customary pricing; vocational and economic experts translate functional limits into dollars.

**Practice points**

* Tie every dollar to **source documents**: coded bills, pay records, tax returns, receipts, or replacement‑services logs.  
* Convert symptoms into **functional loss** (duties performed under duress, abandoned hobbies) corroborated by before‑and‑after witnesses and pain journals using specific descriptors (e.g., burning, stabbing, throbbing).  
* Use **visuals**—surgical photos, medical illustrations, and scarring images—to convey harm quickly.

*Example—Household Services Loss:* A self‑employed stylist with a dominant‑hand wrist ORIF cannot lift her toddler or style hair for months. Counsel documents paid childcare, cleaning services, and lost clientele via booking‑software exports and client letters. A vocational expert explains reduced throughput after return; the economist quantifies productivity loss and household services.

**Practice Note (Damages Buildout)**  
• Maintain a running **specials ledger** with ICD‑10/CPT codes and provider balances.  
• Convert pain to **functional limits** (lift limits, stairs, standing tolerance, sleep disruption).  
• Collect **before/after witnesses** from home, work, and social circles.  
• Price **future care** with reasonable & customary rates and cite the source.  
• Include 3–5 labeled **visuals** (surgical photos, illustrations, scarring) up front.

### **Insurer Conduct and Bad Faith**

Most jurisdictions require insurers to evaluate and settle claims fairly when liability is clear and damages exceed limits. Time‑limited, policy‑limits demands can create excess‑exposure risk and trigger conflicts between insurer and insured. Some states require specific prerequisites (e.g., civil‑remedy notices) before a bad‑faith action.

**Best practices**

* Serve a **clear, complete demand** with adequate time, proof of liability and damages, and precise performance terms (payee info, acceptable release language).  
* Avoid traps: vague deadlines, hidden conditions, or disproportionate demands undermine credibility.  
* Preserve the record: log communications and follow up in writing. Where required, file the appropriate statutory notice before alleging bad faith.

*Example—Limits Tender Secured:* In a clear DUI rear‑end with facial scarring, counsel serves a 30‑day limits demand attaching the crash report, plea transcript, plastic‑surgery estimate, and itemized medicals, and specifies release terms. The carrier tenders on day 21 to protect its insured from excess exposure and potential bad‑faith consequences.

**Practice Note (Time‑Limited Demands & Bad‑Faith Record)**  
• Use a **clear deadline** (commonly 30 days) and spell out acceptance steps (payee, release form).  
• Attach essentials: crash report, key medicals, **itemized bills**, scarring photos/estimates, wage docs.  
• Request **limits and endorsements** in writing; log delivery and receipt.  
• If defects/silence: send a brief **cure letter**; then litigate or file the jurisdiction‑required notice.  
• Avoid gotchas—credibility beats traps.

### **Automated Valuation Systems and Value Drivers**

Most major carriers now standardize injury valuations with Colossus‑type tools (e.g., ClaimIQ, Claims Outcome Advisor), which have largely displaced bespoke, adjuster‑only evaluations. Practitioner sources report these tools are calibrated to undervalue by roughly **12–20%**, which—combined with data‑entry errors and time pressure—can materially depress early offers.

**Typical platform constraints (vary by carrier):**

* The program awards **severity points** against large libraries (on the order of **\~720 diagnoses** and **\~12,500 factors**), with the dollar value per point increasing as total points rise.  
* Only the **first \~60 factors** entered from a demand package may be captured/weighted by the adjuster’s interface.  
* A **limited number of diagnoses (often up to \~7)** can be coded per claim.  
* Data‑entry accuracy by busy adjusters has been reported as **extremely poor** (some trainings even cite **“0% accuracy”** in historical audits), so clean, code‑rich submissions matter.

**Demand engineering for software:**

* Put the **strongest 60 value drivers first**; cluster related items; use bold subheads and short, copy‑and‑paste‑ready bullets.  
* Place **ICD‑10/CPT codes** next to each bill/diagnosis and include impairment ratings when available to reduce transcription errors.  
* Follow with a one‑page, **supervisor‑ready** summary anticipating roundtable questions and authority requests.

**High‑leverage value drivers:**

* **Statutory violations** (speeding, stop‑sign, overweight loads) supporting negligence per se.  
* **Aggravated conduct** (DUI/DWI) supporting punitive exposure and often non‑dischargeable in bankruptcy.  
* **Scars/disfigurement**, especially highly visible areas; include plastic‑surgery estimates. (Some evaluators weigh facial/leg scars on women and chest keloids on men more heavily.)  
* **Objective pain indicators** (e.g., documented spikes in blood pressure; consistent pain‑scale location/severity charting in treating notes).  
* **Loss of function/hobbies** and **“duties under duress”** tied to the client’s actual life and locality.  
* **Psychological injuries** (PTSD, anxiety, depression) with treatment history and future‑care costs.  
* **Life expectancy/age** for permanent harms (younger clients tend to score higher).  
* **Attorney profiling/track record**; platforms and carriers track firms’ trial readiness and negotiation history and surface “negotiation advice” to adjusters.  
* **Non‑software injuries**: roughly two dozen catastrophic categories (death, major burns, spinal cord/brain injury, paralysis, CRPS, etc.) are typically escalated for **human valuation**—flag these explicitly.

*Example—From Software Ceiling to Human Review:* A young teacher sustains a mild TBI with cognitive fatigue. Counsel’s demand leads with neuropsychological testing, accommodations required to continue teaching, and collateral statements from the principal and spouse. The adjuster escalates the file out of software to a human valuation panel, resulting in a materially higher offer.

**Practice Note (Colossus & AI Valuation — numbers at a glance)**  
• Replaces most adjuster‑only evaluations.  
• Calibrated undervaluation: **12–20%**.  
• Libraries: **\~720 diagnoses / \~12,500 factors**; severity‑point model with progressive point value.  
• Data‑capture constraints: **first \~60 factors** and **\~7 diagnoses** commonly entered; order matters.  
• Data‑entry reliability: training audits have reported **near‑zero accuracy** without code‑rich submissions.  
• Human‑review categories: ≈**24 catastrophic** injuries (death, SCI/TBI, major burns, paralysis, CRPS, etc.).  
• Attorney profiling: carriers track trial readiness and prior outcomes.

**Psych → Numbers (make psychological harm count)**

1. Use **DSM‑5/ICD‑10 codes** (e.g., **F43.10** PTSD; **F33.1** MDD; **F41.1** GAD; **F43.23** Adjustment Disorder).  
2. Include **CPT codes** for treatment (e.g., **90792** psych eval; **90834/90837** psychotherapy; **99213/99214** med management).  
3. Attach **standardized scales**: **PCL‑5** (PTSD), **PHQ‑9** (depression), **GAD‑7** (anxiety) with baseline and latest scores.  
4. Document **work/role restrictions** and **“duties under duress”** with third‑party corroboration.  
5. Price **future care** (therapy frequency/duration, meds, EMDR/CBT, psychiatry follow‑ups) using reasonable & customary rates; roll into the life‑care plan.  
6. For software: place the psych diagnosis, scales, treatment frequency, and functional limits **within the first 60 items**.

   ### **Professional Responsibility: Truthfulness, Confidentiality, Civility, and Third‑Party Funding**

Zealous negotiation is bounded by candor (no false statements of material fact), confidentiality (protect client information; use HIPAA‑compliant authorizations), and communication duties (obtain and document informed consent). Civility—meeting deadlines, honoring commitments, and treating adjusters respectfully—enhances long‑term effectiveness. Be cautious when interacting with litigation‑funding companies; without written client authorization, share only public information.

*Example—Credibility Preserved:* After settlement, a provider mails a duplicate $1,200 refund. Counsel discloses the error and returns it. Months later, the same adjuster increases an unrelated offer after noting counsel’s reliability in the claims notes.

## **Practical Applications / Procedures**

### **Early Case Development: Establishing Liability**

**First 10‑Day Plan**

1. Send preservation letters to carriers, tow yards, and businesses for EDR data and video.  
2. Deploy an investigator to photograph sightlines, signage, grades, skid marks, debris fields, and undercarriages.  
3. Canvas for third‑party witnesses and doorbell cameras.  
4. Order 911 audio and traffic‑court files; capture sworn municipal‑court testimony where available; calendar statutes and municipal hearings.  
5. Identify all potential defendants (e.g., negligent maintenance contractor, cargo loader, premises owner).

*Example—Hidden Defendant Found:* In a dump‑truck rollover, the truck was under weight limits, but the load was negligently secured by a third‑party loader. Spill‑pattern photos and bills of lading support adding the loader, unlocking additional coverage and settlement authority.

**Practice Note (Scene & Evidence Capture)**  
• Send **video preservation** letters to adjacent businesses (30–90‑day overwrite).  
• Re‑create **time‑of‑day** photos for lighting and sightlines.  
• Photograph **undercarriage/seat tracks/bumper beam** in MIST cases.  
• Identify **additional defendants** (maintenance, loader, premises, municipality) early to unlock coverage.

### **Building the Damages Record**

Create a **medical chronology** keyed to complaints, objective findings, functional limits, and work restrictions. Obtain treating‑physician letters on causation, permanence, restrictions, and future care. For long‑term deficits, retain a life‑care planner, vocational expert, and economist. Encourage a contemporaneous pain/activity journal using specific descriptors.

*Example—Subjective Pain Made Concrete:* A warehouse worker with chronic lumbar pain returns to work with restrictions. The demand includes PT lift‑test results, supervisor affidavits about modified duties, and missed‑overtime schedules. The combination converts subjective pain into measurable functional loss.

**Practice Note (Chronology & Treating Opinions)**  
• Build a 4‑column **chronology**: Date | Provider | Key findings/complaints | **Functional limits**.  
• Request addenda with **causation/permanency** language if missing.  
• Explain **gaps in care** (insurance, childcare, transportation).  
• Get **written work restrictions** with start/end dates.

### **Managing Liens, First‑Party Benefits, and Public Programs**

Inventory health‑plan, ERISA, workers’ compensation, Medicaid, Medicare, PIP, and med‑pay interests early. Verify lien validity and scope, request itemizations and EOBs, and negotiate reductions. Analyze fee‑schedule limits and anti‑subrogation rules. In Medicare cases, resolve conditional payments and evaluate whether a Medicare Set‑Aside is warranted for future allocation. Before disbursement, check for bankruptcies and child‑support intercepts.

*Example—Lien Strategy Adds Net Recovery:* A $120,000 ERISA plan asserts full reimbursement. Counsel obtains plan documents showing equitable, not legal, relief; negotiates a 40% reduction by documenting limited funds and procurement costs; and structures part of the settlement for future care, increasing the client’s net by five figures.

**Practice Note (Liens & Benefits Workflow)**  
• Create a **lien inventory**: Plan, Amount Paid, Asserted, Basis (ERISA/state), Reduction theory, Status.  
• Request **SPD/plan docs** early; analyze make‑whole/common‑fund.  
• Run **Medicaid/Medicare** portals; resolve conditionals; consider **MSA** triggers.  
• No disbursement without **final lien letters**; file reductions in the closing packet.

### **Crafting the Demand Package**

Treat the demand as the adjuster’s briefing to supervisors and software.

**Suggested Table of Contents**

1. Liability overview with exhibits list.  
2. Medical chronology and itemized bills (with ICD/CPT codes).  
3. Future care (treating‑physician opinion, life‑care plan).  
4. Wage loss and capacity (employer letters, W‑2/1099, vocational/economic reports).  
5. Non‑economic harms (before‑and‑after statements, photographs, journals).  
6. Lien and benefit summary.  
7. Settlement demand and payment terms (including release requirements).

**Software‑aware formatting**: many platforms capture only the **first \~60 factors** and **limit diagnoses (often to \~7)**; lead with the most potent entries, and place **ICD/CPT codes** next to each item to reduce transcription errors.

*Example—Video Brochure Moves the Needle:* For a client with prominent facial scarring, counsel adds a three‑minute settlement video with surgeon narration and day‑in‑the‑life footage. The adjuster cites the video in the authority request; the offer increases 35% from the pre‑video number.

### **Negotiation Playbook: Timing, Posture, and Pressure**

Calibrate timing to reserves, statutes, and record completeness. Use **structured movement** (e.g., reasoned brackets) rather than arbitrary haggling. Ask for written authority ranges when appropriate, and escalate when non‑responsiveness appears systemic. Consider tolling agreements if limitations are near but records are still developing. In some jurisdictions, filing in federal court can facilitate policy‑limit disclosure through initial disclosures.

*Example—Bracket to Break Impasse:* Carrier at $90k; plaintiff at $300k. Counsel proposes a $175k–$250k bracket, signaling willingness to resolve in that corridor if the carrier moves to the lower end. The adjuster secures higher authority and lands at $205k the same day.

**Practice Note (Brackets & Movement)**  
• Move in **decreasing increments** as proof saturates; explain each move.  
• Use mirrored brackets to signal a **zone of potential agreement**.  
• Ask whether the adjuster has **sought higher authority** and when the next meeting is set.  
• Calendar **quarter/year‑end** and internal meeting dates to time proposals.

### **Bargaining & Concessions**

Use principled movement and documented proof to create pressure while preserving credibility.

* **Demand quality**: Present an indexed, bound demand with complete exhibits and **medical codes**; this becomes the adjuster’s brief to supervisors and the software.  
* **Lowball offers (\< medical specials)**: “Paper the file.” Serve updated physician statements on permanency, future arthritis, and restrictions; then **ask the adjuster to explain—preferably in writing—how an offer below specials is justified** in light of the record.  
* **Non‑responsive adjusters**: First **verify the reason** (e.g., leave or reassignment) with management; if the issue is chronic overload or neglect, **file suit** and push for **early mediation** to trigger assignment to a litigation adjuster with higher authority.  
* **Pre‑existing injuries**: Prove resolution prior to the crash or **aggravation** after, using before/after MRIs and treating‑physician opinions.  
* **Coverage problems**: Avoid pleading intentional conduct that may void coverage; monitor and address **reservation‑of‑rights** letters, especially in multi‑party construction and premises cases.  
* **Policy limits**: Discover early (insurance‑search vendors; targeted interrogatories; in some venues, **federal filing** prompts disclosure in initial disclosures). Avoid asking for limits on **very small claims**—it costs credibility.  
* **Strategic concessions**: In disputed‑liability jurisdictions with comparative negligence, calibrated concessions can unlock mid‑range settlements without collapsing non‑economic value.  
* **Walkaway strategy (settlement)**: If the carrier ignores documented value, be prepared to try the case. **Risk management** sometimes favors accepting a guaranteed, reasonable resolution over “full ask” to avoid the variability of a jury.  
* **Walkaway strategy (representation)**: If a client lies about **material facts**, withdrawal may be necessary.  
* **Annuities & apologies**: Propose structured settlements for minors/large futures and consider non‑monetary terms (apologies) where emotionally important.

*Examples*

* *Offer Below Specials:* After submitting updated impairment ratings and a future‑care letter, counsel asks the adjuster to memorialize the basis for an offer below medical specials. The request prompts supervisor review and a revised offer above specials.  
* *Non‑Responsive File:* After confirming the assigned adjuster is on leave, counsel escalates to a manager, then files suit and sets early mediation. A litigation adjuster with higher authority resolves the case within the new bracket.

  ### **Countering Common Adjuster Arguments**

Addressing standard defenses early—in writing—prevents entrenchment and equips the adjuster to justify movement.

* **“Low impact can’t cause injury.”** Emphasize human vulnerability vs. vehicle stiffness; provide undercarriage photos and treating‑doctor causation letters; use biomechanical analysis when value justifies.  
  *Example:* Minimal bumper damage but bent brackets and seat‑track deformation; treating note ties annular tear to acceleration forces.  
* **“Injuries are subjective.”** Cite jury instructions that pain and suffering resist exact measurement; corroborate with journals, medication logs, therapy notes, and before‑and‑after witnesses.  
  *Example:* Sleep logs and spouse testimony align with increased dosage of neuropathic medications.  
* **“Alternative cause (workout/aging).”** Show pre‑incident baseline and resolution of prior complaints; obtain treating‑physician opinions distinguishing acute changes.  
  *Example:* Pre‑ and post‑MRI comparison identifies new annular tear vs. preexisting degeneration.  
* **“Failure to mitigate/follow advice.”** Document medical reasoning for treatment choices and work restrictions.  
  *Example:* Injection declined due to uncontrolled diabetes per endocrinology note.  
* **“Bills/treatment unreasonable.”** Link tests to ruled‑out diagnoses; note that legal responsibility runs to the billed amount; provide provider affidavits on reasonableness and necessity.  
  *Example:* ER CT ordered to exclude intracranial bleed given reported LOC and anticoagulant status.  
* **“Not out of pocket (collateral sources).”** In strong collateral‑source jurisdictions, decline to discuss third‑party payments and focus on value of services.  
  *Example:* Negotiation letter cites governing jury instruction and pivots to provider charges and outcome.  
* **“No witnesses.”** Stress that quality, not count, of evidence governs; rely on physical evidence and contemporaneous complaints.  
  *Example:* Skid‑mark analysis and 911 call timing establish fault sequence.  
* **“Policy/limits won’t cover that.””** Request written proof of limits, declarations, and endorsements; tie refusal to settle within limits to potential bad‑faith exposure.  
  *Example:* Carrier produces umbrella dec page; within‑limits demand prompts prompt tender after roundtable.

**Practice Note (Pre‑buttals in the Demand)**  
• Add a one‑page **“Anticipated Defenses & Responses”** section with a cite to jury instructions.  
• Include a **treating quote** on causation/permanency and **one exhibit** per defense.  
• Offer targeted **stipulations** to blunt fishing expeditions.

### **Escalation: Litigation, Mediation, and Structures**

File suit when offers ignore documented value, discovery is necessary, or policy‑limit disclosure requires litigation. Use mediation once records and expert opinions are mature. Consider structured settlements—especially for minors or substantial futures—to reconcile present‑value constraints with lifetime needs. Non‑monetary terms, including apologies, can sometimes help bridge gaps.

*Example—Litigation Adjuster Unlocks Authority:* A pre‑suit offer stalls at $45k against $62k specials and a surgery recommendation. Filing triggers assignment to a litigation adjuster with $150k authority; early mediation resolves at $165k with a partial structure funding future injections.

**Practice Note (When to Litigate/Mediating Well)**  
• File when value drivers are ignored, discovery is needed, or limits are opaque.  
• **Mediation brief**: ≤10 pages; decisive exhibits only; propose brackets in advance.  
• Bring a draft **term sheet** (structure options, Medicare language, confidentiality).

### **Client Communication, Authority, and Expectation Management**

Educate clients about value drivers, venue effects, timelines, and the difference between gross and net recovery. Provide **net‑to‑client worksheets** with every material offer. Document specific settlement authority and confirm by email or signed form. Reset expectations shaped by anecdotes and advertising.

*Example—Expectations Reset:* A client expects $250k based on a neighbor’s story. Counsel presents the chronology, comparable verdicts for the venue, lien projections, and a net‑to‑client sheet at multiple settlement amounts. The client authorizes $110k–$140k; the case settles at $135k without regret.

**Practice Note (Client Authority & Net Sheets)**  
• Provide a **net‑to‑client** at every offer tier; get initials.  
• Confirm authority in writing (subject: **\[AUTHORITY\] $\_\_ floor / $\_\_ target**).  
• Summarize **risks/alternatives** in plain language; attach verdict snapshots.

### **Settlement Disbursement and Trust Accounting**

Settlement funds must be handled through the attorney trust account (IOLTA or equivalent). Delay disbursement until liens are verified and resolved; confirm bankruptcy status and child‑support obligations; review release language for hidden indemnity or confidentiality traps; and document the final net‑to‑client calculation.

*Example—Clean Closeout:* Before cutting checks, counsel confirms no active bankruptcy via PACER, secures Medicaid’s final lien letter after Ahlborn‑style allocation, and obtains provider reductions in writing. The closing statement itemizes fees, costs, liens, and net to client, signed by the client and filed to the trust account ledger.

**Practice Note (Disbursement & Trust)**  
• No checks until **final lien letters** arrive; three‑way reconciliation complete.  
• Record all payouts in the **trust ledger**; keep reduction letters and releases in closing packet.  
• Send a **closing letter** with check, signed statement, release, and tax/Medicare notices as applicable.

    ## **Challenges and Pitfalls**

    ### **Disorganized or Incomplete Proof**

Gaps in treatment, missing bills, or unsourced estimates depress value and credibility. Build a closed record before demanding top dollar.

*Example—Chronology Cure:* An initial $18k offer jumps to $52k after counsel delivers a paginated, indexed binder with complete coded bills, a medical chronology, and employer verification of missed time.

### **Data‑Entry and Automation Traps**

Software often captures only early entries and may mis‑code diagnoses. Reduce transcription errors by labeling diagnoses and placing the most powerful value drivers first.

*Example—Code It Right:* An adjuster initially ignores radiculopathy because notes lacked ICD coding. Counsel resubmits with ICD‑10 and CPT references on page one; the severity score increases and authority rises.

### **Miscalibrated Demands and Credibility Risk**

Anchors must be defensible. Overreaching undermines trust; undervaluing early suppresses reserves.

*Example—Defensible Anchor:* Instead of a round $500k ask, counsel presents $468,300 with line‑item support and a principled non‑economic range. The specificity helps the supervisor approve six‑figure movement.

### **Causation and Mitigation Attacks**

Expect arguments about alternative causes or failure to follow medical advice.

*Example—Mitigation Documented:* Client declined an injection due to poorly controlled diabetes. Endocrinology notes and physician explanation show the choice was medically reasonable, blunting mitigation arguments.

### **Social Media, Statements, and Inconsistencies**

Posts and prior statements can be weaponized if unmanaged.

*Example—Context Matters:* A smiling wedding photo is explained by a pain‑journal entry and testimony about leaving early due to back spasms, neutralizing insinuations.

### **Collateral Source and Coverage Issues**

Know the collateral‑source rule, med‑pay offsets, and policy language; avoid coverage‑defeating theories unless strategic.

*Example—Coverage Preserved:* Counsel avoids pleading intentional assault where evidence is shaky, proceeding on negligence to maintain indemnity and maximize collectible coverage.

### **Lien and Benefit Hazards**

Validate and negotiate liens; missteps can erase gains.

*Example—Medicaid Allocation:* Counsel uses an Ahlborn‑style allocation (where permitted) to reduce Medicaid reimbursement proportionally to the settlement’s medical‑expense component, increasing the client’s net recovery.

### **Ethical Failures**

Misrepresentation or unauthorized disclosure creates discipline and malpractice risk. Withdraw when honesty is impossible.

*Example—Withdrawal Done Right:* After discovering the client lied about a pre‑accident claim, counsel withdraws, documents reasons consistent with confidentiality, and avoids compounding the misconduct.

## **Conclusion / Key Takeaways**

* Design proof to influence **reserves, software, and human reviewers** simultaneously.  
* **Front‑load** high‑value facts; time demands around statutes, discovery needs, and fiscal‑year dynamics.  
* Convert symptoms into **functional loss** backed by treating‑physician opinions and collateral witnesses.  
* Build **supervisor‑ready** demands: organized exhibits, coded bills, prioritized value drivers, and a defensible anchor.  
* Use **structured negotiation** tools (brackets, reasoned concessions, mediation) and escalate to litigation when offers ignore documented value.  
* Manage **liens, first‑party benefits, and coverage** proactively; obtain written confirmation of limits and avoid theories that forfeit indemnity absent a plan.  
* Protect **credibility and confidentiality**; document client authority and net‑to‑client outcomes; maintain civility.  
* Visible **trial readiness** and a credible willingness to walk away remain the strongest levers for fair settlement.

  ## **Glossary**

**Adjuster Authority** — The maximum dollar amount an insurance adjuster may approve for settlement without supervisor approval, typically tiered by experience and role.

**Aggravation of Injury** — Worsening of a pre‑existing condition by a new incident; the defendant is liable for the aggravation even if a completely new permanent injury is not present.

**Anchoring** — A negotiation tactic that sets a high reference point, often by leading with substantial economic damages, to influence perceived claim value.

**Annuity (Settlement)** — A financial product funding periodic payments from a lump‑sum settlement, commonly used for minors or substantial future‑care needs.

**Artificial Intelligence (AI) in Claims** — Programs such as Colossus, ClaimIQ, and Claims Outcome Advisor that standardize claim valuation, often producing conservative ranges.

**Bad Faith Law** — Statutory or common‑law duties requiring carriers to handle claims fairly and reasonably; violations may expose carriers to damages beyond policy limits.

**Black Box (EDR)** — Vehicle event‑data recorders capturing pre‑ and post‑impact metrics (speed, braking, delta‑v), useful for reconstruction.

**Collateral Source Rule** — A doctrine that bars defendants from reducing liability by pointing to third‑party payments (e.g., health insurance) for the same losses.

**Colossus** — A widely used valuation platform that assigns severity points to diagnoses and factors to generate ranges for settlement negotiation.

**Comparative Negligence** — Allocation of fault between plaintiff and defendant, reducing recovery by the plaintiff’s percentage of fault.

**Confidentiality (Attorney–Client)** — The duty to protect information relating to representation unless the client consents or a recognized exception applies.

**Concession (Strategic)** — Deliberate, limited compromise to achieve movement or resolution, commonly used in mixed‑fault cases.

**Contributory Negligence** — A minority doctrine barring recovery if the plaintiff is even minimally at fault in causing the injury.

**Crashworthiness** — Product‑liability concept imposing liability for enhanced injuries caused by a vehicle’s defective design during a crash.

**CRN (Civil Remedy Notice)** — A jurisdiction‑specific notice that may be required as a precondition to a bad‑faith action against an insurer.

**Discretionary Costs** — Litigation expenses that may be awarded to the prevailing party depending on jurisdictional rules (e.g., expert fees, deposition costs).

**Duties Under Duress (Colossus)** — A value driver capturing activities a claimant can perform only with pain or difficulty.

**Economic Damages** — Tangible, quantifiable losses such as medical bills (past/future), lost wages, diminished earning capacity, and out‑of‑pocket costs.

**Eggshell Skull Doctrine** — The defendant takes the plaintiff as found and is liable for full consequences, even if unusually severe due to pre‑existing fragility.

**EOB (Explanation of Benefits)** — Insurer statement detailing amounts billed, allowed, paid, and owed for medical services.

**ERISA Policy** — Employer health plan governed by federal law; plan language controls reimbursement rights and defenses.

**Ethical Red Flags** — Indicators of potential professional‑conduct violations (e.g., client dishonesty, concealment of material facts).

**Fact Finder (Attorney as)** — The attorney’s role in gathering, organizing, and presenting reliable information to decision‑makers.

**Fee‑Scheduled Amounts** — Statutory or contractual caps on reimbursable medical charges that can influence evidence and negotiations.

**Hartford Letter** — A within‑limits demand that warns of excess exposure if the insurer fails to settle; often triggers insurer–insured conflicts and separate counsel for the insured.

**IME (Independent Medical Examination)** — Evaluation by a defense‑selected physician to challenge diagnosis, causation, or treatment.

**Informed Consent (Client)** — Client authorization for a proposed course (e.g., accepting an offer) after disclosure of risks, benefits, and alternatives.

**IOLTA (Trust Account)** — Interest on Lawyers’ Trust Accounts program for client funds held in trust pending disbursement.

**Keloid Scarring** — Raised, thickened scarring; prominent scars are significant value drivers, especially when visible (face, legs, chest).

**Letter of Protection (LOP)** — Provider agreement to await payment from settlement proceeds in exchange for ongoing treatment.

**Life‑Care Planner** — Expert who projects future medical, rehab, and attendant‑care needs and costs for catastrophically injured clients.

**Litigation Adjuster** — Claims professional handling suits; typically armed with higher authority than pre‑suit adjusters.

**Low‑Balling** — Offering significantly below reasonable value to test resolve or exploit urgency.

**Medicare Set‑Aside (MSA)** — Allocation of settlement funds to future injury‑related care otherwise payable by Medicare.

**Minimal Impact Soft Tissue (MIST)** — Label used to discount claims where property damage appears minor despite real human injury.

**Moeller Counsel** — Separate counsel for an insured when conflicts arise with the insurer (terminology varies by jurisdiction).

**Narrative (Claim)** — The cohesive story connecting fault, medical causation, and human loss.

**Non‑Economic Damages** — Intangible harms: pain, suffering, disfigurement, loss of enjoyment/consortium, and emotional distress.

**Nuclear Verdicts** — Extremely large verdicts that far exceed offers or expectations; often discussed in defense strategy.

**PACER** — Federal electronic records system used, among other things, to screen for client bankruptcies before disbursement.

**Pain Multiplier** — Heuristic sometimes used to estimate non‑economic damages as a multiple of economic losses; less relevant where carriers rely on software.

**PIP (Personal Injury Protection)** — No‑fault coverage paying medical expenses and, sometimes, wage loss regardless of fault.

**Puffery** — Non‑factual negotiation posturing that stops short of misrepresentation.

**Punitive Damages** — Damages intended to punish egregious conduct and deter similar behavior.

**Recorded Statement** — An adjuster‑recorded interview of a claimant or witness, often used to probe inconsistencies.

**Reserves (Claim)** — The insurer’s internal estimate of ultimate payout used for financial reporting and authority setting.

**Statute of Limitations** — Deadline for filing suit; often subject to tolling or special rules (e.g., minors, wrongful death).

**Subrogation** — An insurer’s right to recoup amounts paid from the responsible third party or its carrier.

**Tolling Agreement** — Contract pausing limitations to allow further negotiation or investigation.

**Trial Guides** — A publisher and education resource for plaintiff practitioners on PI practice and trial skills.

**Trust Account (Attorney)** — Separate account for client funds distinct from operating funds; strict records are required.

**Value Drivers (Colossus)** — Inputs that increase software scores: objective findings, documented limitations, scarring, aggravated conduct, and more.

**Venue** — The court and community from which a jury is drawn; materially affects case value.

**Vocational Expert** — Specialist who assesses work capacity and earning impact tied to injury.

**Walkaway Strategy** — Signaling readiness to litigate if reasonable terms are not reached; a key leverage point.

**Workers’ Compensation Lien** — Reimbursement claim by a comp carrier against a third‑party recovery for benefits paid.
